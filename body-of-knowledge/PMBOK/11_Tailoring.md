# 11 — Tailoring

> *Source: PMBOK® Guide — Eighth Edition by PMI, §3 Tailoring (pp. 103–112)*

## Purpose

Tailoring is the deliberate adaptation of the project management approach, governance, and processes to align with the project's unique environment and objectives. It involves analyzing and modifying the development approach, life cycle, processes, and stakeholder engagement to better fit the project context and intended outcomes. Tailoring is not a one-time exercise — it is a continuous activity of inspection and adaptation driven by the guiding [[04_Governance_Performance_Domain|project management principles]].

## Why Tailor?

There is no single project management approach that suits all projects all of the time. The structure used to deliver projects can be extensive or minimal, rigorous or lightweight, and comprehensive or streamlined. Variables that influence the need for tailoring include:

- **Project criticality** — a nuclear reactor requires far more rigor than an office building
- **Scale and duration** — communication needs differ vastly between a 10-member team and a 200-member team
- **Complexity, industry standards, organizational culture**
- **Number of stakeholders and organizational PM maturity**

Too few processes lead to ineffective management; too many are costly and wasteful. Effective tailoring delivers:

- More commitment from team members who helped design the approach
- Enhanced customer-oriented focus
- More efficient use of project resources

## What to Tailor

Three aspects of a project can be tailored:

### Life Cycle and Development Approach

Selecting the life cycle and its phases is itself a tailoring activity. A single project may combine approaches — for example, a new data center might use a **predictive** approach for construction and an **adaptive** approach for computing capabilities, resulting in a **hybrid** approach at the project level.

### Processes

Process tailoring determines which elements should be:

| Action | Purpose |
|--------|---------|
| **Added** | Address unique conditions or increase rigor |
| **Modified** | Better fit project/team needs (adjust inputs, outputs, tools & techniques) |
| **Removed** | Reduce unnecessary cost or effort |
| **Blended** | Combine elements for added value |
| **Aligned** | Ensure consistency in definition and application |

### Engagement

Tailoring engagement for the people involved includes:

- **People** — Assessing skills and capabilities of the project leadership and team, ensuring they adapt to evolving needs.
- **Empowerment** — Choosing which responsibilities and local decision-making authority to delegate to the project team. High-capability teams may warrant high empowerment; other situations call for more supervision.
- **Integration** — Creating one cohesive project team from diverse contributors (contractors, channel partners, external entities, and internal staff).

## The Tailoring Process

Tailoring follows a four-step cycle that begins before the project and continues throughout its life:

### Step 1: Select Initial Development Approach

Determine whether a **predictive**, **adaptive**, or **hybrid** approach is best suited for the endeavor. Project teams apply their knowledge of the product, delivery cadence, and available options. A **suitability filter** — not a rigid method but a decision-making tool — helps evaluate the project's characteristics against criteria of culture, team dynamics, and project factors to guide the initial selection.

### Step 2: Tailor for the Organization

Organizations may provide methodologies and development approach guides as starting points. This step involves adding, removing, and reconfiguring elements to make the approach suitable for the individual organization. Key considerations:

- Align tailoring with organizational process governance and policies.
- Consider the context of the **program and/or portfolio** within which the project operates.
- The **PMO** (Project Management Office) plays a vital role in reviewing and approving tailored approaches, especially when tailoring affects external groups. Tailoring that impacts only the project team typically requires only project manager approval.
- In **multiorganization projects**, integration planning must harmonize tailored practices across participating organizations.

### Step 3: Tailor for the Project

Three key attribute categories guide project-level tailoring:

**Product/Deliverable considerations:**
- Standards compliance, type of product, industry/market, technology stability, timeframe, stability of requirements, security, and feasibility of incremental/iterative delivery.

**Project Team considerations:**
- Team size, geography (colocated vs. remote), organizational distribution, team experience, access to customer feedback, and team diversity.

**Culture considerations:**
- Buy-in for the proposed approach, trust in the team's capability, empowerment levels, and alignment of organizational values with the project approach.

### Step 4: Implement Ongoing Improvement

Tailoring is not a one-time exercise. During progressive elaboration, review points, phase gates, and retrospectives provide opportunities to **inspect and adapt** the process, development approach, and delivery frequency. Keeping the team engaged with process improvement fosters pride of ownership and demonstrates a commitment to quality and innovation.

## Tailoring the Performance Domains

The work associated with each of the eight [[04_Governance_Performance_Domain|project performance domains]] can also be tailored based on project uniqueness. The **principles of project management** guide practitioner behavior as they tailor each domain — including **Stakeholders**, **Team**, **Development Approach and Life Cycle**, **Planning**, **Project Work**, **Delivery**, **Measurement**, and **Uncertainty** — to the project context and environment.

## Diagnostics

Periodic reviews (retrospectives, lessons learned) help assess whether current approaches are effective. Teams without retrospectives can rely on issues, threats, quality metrics, and stakeholder feedback. The following table provides common situations and suggested tailoring responses.

| Situation | Tailoring Suggestion |
|-----------|----------------------|
| Deliverables are poor quality | Analyze root causes and implement targeted feedback and quality assurance measures. Focus on **process improvements** rather than just increasing verification steps. |
| Team members are unsure how to proceed | Assess specific team needs and provide appropriate support: guidance, mentorship, training, and knowledge-sharing practices. |
| Team members are not cooperative; working in silos | Conduct team-building activities and individual meetings to understand causes. Strengthen bonds and promote coordination. |
| Long delays waiting for approvals | Streamline the approval process by appointing people authorized to make decisions up to certain value thresholds. |
| Too much work in progress and/or waste | Use **value stream mapping** and **kanban boards** to visualize work, identify issues, and find solutions. |
| Stakeholders are not engaged or share negative feedback | Assess whether stakeholders receive timely and relevant information. Tailor communication methods to balance simplicity with deeper engagement. |
| Lack of transparency about project progress | Ensure appropriate data is collected, analyzed, shared, and discussed. Validate agreement on measures with the team and stakeholders. |
| Issues/risks keep surfacing unprepared | Explore root causes for gaps in project processes or activities that leave the team reacting rather than progressing. |

## Summary

Tailoring adapts the approach, governance, and processes to fit the project's environment and objectives. The four-step tailoring process — **Select Initial Development Approach → Tailor for the Organization → Tailor for the Project → Implement Ongoing Improvement** — is led by project stakeholders with organizational guidelines ensuring alignment. Tailoring is not just about making changes, but about verifying and validating that the tailored approaches are effective and aligned with project outcomes.
