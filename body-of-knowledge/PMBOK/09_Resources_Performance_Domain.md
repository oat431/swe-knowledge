# Resources Performance Domain

> *Source: PMBOK® Guide — Eighth Edition by PMI, §2.6 Resources Performance Domain (pp. 79–92)*

## Purpose

The Resources performance domain addresses how effectively and efficiently a project team plans for and utilizes its available resources — encompassing both **human resources** (the project team) and **physical or virtual resources** (equipment, materials, supplies, facilities, infrastructure, software, testing environments, licenses, services, information, documents). It also covers resource availability, utilization, and maintenance. The goal is to ensure the right resources are available to the project manager and project team at the right time and place.

## Key Concepts

- **Project Manager.** As leader of the project team, the project manager is responsible for team formation and operation as an effective group. This includes acquiring, managing, motivating, and empowering the team; developing team skills and competencies; maintaining awareness of team environment, geographical locations, communications, organizational change management, politics, and cultural issues; and modeling professional and ethical behavior.

- **Resource Manager.** An individual with management authority over one or more resources. The project team may not have direct control over resource selection due to collective bargaining agreements, subcontractor personnel, matrix environments, or internal/external reporting relationships. Internal resources are acquired (assigned) from functional or resource managers; external resources are obtained through partnerships, joint ventures, or procurement processes (see [[Appendix_A3_Procurement]]).

- **Human Resources (Project Team Members).** Individuals with assigned roles and responsibilities who work collectively toward a shared project goal. Involvement of all team members in planning and decision-making strengthens commitment. Teams may be guided by top-down leadership (centralized management) or may self-organize with decentralized decision-making (distributed management) — see [[13_Tools_and_Techniques]].

- **Physical, Material, or Virtual Resources.** Any non-person resource needed for project completion: equipment, materials, supplies, facilities, infrastructure, software, testing environments, licenses, services, etc. Organizations need sufficient data on resource demands (current and future), required configurations, and supply. Large-scale projects may require significant procurement activities (see [[Appendix_A3_Procurement]]).

## Processes

The Resources performance domain includes five processes that span planning through monitoring and controlling:

### 1. Plan Resource Management

The process of defining how to estimate, acquire, lead, and utilize physical, virtual, and team resources. Its key benefit is establishing the approach and level of management effort needed based on project type and complexity. Performed once or at predefined points.

**Key Inputs:** Project charter, project management plan (quality management plan, scope baseline), project documents (project schedule, assumption log, requirements documentation, risk register, stakeholder register), enterprise environmental factors, organizational process assets.

**Key Tools & Techniques:** Expert judgment, interviews, SWOT analysis, hierarchical charts, responsibility assignment matrix (RAM), text-oriented formats, organizational theory, green human resource management, resource-based view, meetings.

**Key Outputs:** Resource management plan, team charter, project document updates (risk register).

### 2. Estimate Resources

The process of estimating team resources and the type and quantities of physical or virtual resources necessary to perform project work. Its key benefit is identifying the type, quantity, and characteristics of resources required, enabling proactive adjustments for potential shortages or surpluses. Performed once or at predefined points. Closely related to the [[06_Schedule_Performance_Domain]].

**Key Inputs:** Resource management plan, schedule management plan, scope baseline, activity attributes, activity list, assumption log, cost estimates, resource calendars, risk register, project schedule.

**Key Tools & Techniques:** Expert judgment, bottom-up estimating, analogous estimating, parametric estimating, alternative analysis, PMIS, interviews, artificial intelligence, predictive analytics, virtual reality, augmented reality, branch and bound, genetic algorithms, COCOMO (Constructive Cost Model).

**Key Outputs:** Resource requirements, basis of estimates, resource breakdown structure (RBS), project document updates (activity attributes, assumption log, lessons learned register, risk register).

### 3. Acquire Resources

The process of obtaining the team, physical, or virtual resources necessary to complete project work, based on the activity list. Its key benefit is outlining and guiding resource selection and assignment. Performed periodically throughout the project as needed.

**Key Inputs:** Resource management plan, procurement management plan, cost baseline, project schedule, resource calendars, resource requirements, stakeholder register, lessons learned register.

**Key Tools & Techniques:** Multicriteria decision analysis, negotiation, problem-solving, preassignment, virtual teams.

**Key Outputs:** Physical or virtual resource assignments, project team assignments, resource calendars, change requests, project management plan updates (resource management plan), project document updates (project schedule, RBS, resource requirements, risk register, stakeholder register).

**Critical Factors:**
- The project manager must collaborate, negotiate, and influence resource providers.
- Failure to acquire necessary resources may affect schedule, budget, customer satisfaction, and quality, and could result in project cancellation.
- If resources are unavailable due to constraints, alternative resources (with different competencies, features, or costs) may be assigned if risks are acknowledged and legal/regulatory criteria are not violated.
- Impact of resource unavailability must be documented on schedule, budget, risks, quality, and training plans.

### 4. Lead the Team

The process of guiding, developing, and managing the team to enhance performance and achieve project goals through feedback, collaboration, conflict resolution, and escalation. Its key benefit is influencing team behavior, managing conflict, and resolving issues. Combines both management and leadership activities:

- **Management activities:** Focus on means — effective processes, planning, coordinating, measuring, monitoring work.
- **Leadership activities:** Focus on people — influencing, motivating, listening, enabling.

**Common Aspects of Team Development:**
- **Vision and objectives** — Communicated throughout the project; referenced in decision-making and problem-solving.
- **Roles and responsibilities** — Ensure team members understand and fulfill their roles; identify knowledge/skill gaps and address through training, mentoring, or coaching.
- **Project team operations** — Facilitate communication, problem-solving, consensus building; develop team charter and operating guidelines/norms.
- **Guidance** — Directed at the overall team and individual members on specific tasks or deliverables.
- **Growth** — Identify areas of strength and improvement; work collaboratively toward growth goals.

**Characteristics of High-Performing Teams:**
- Open communication — environment that fosters safe, productive dialogue.
- Shared understanding — common purpose and alignment with organizational mission.
- Shared ownership — team members feel ownership over outcomes.
- Trust — willingness to perform at higher levels.
- Collaboration — working as a team rather than in silos or competitively.
- Adaptability — ability to adapt work methods to environment or situation.
- Resilience — quick recovery from issues or failures.
- Empowerment, delegation, and autonomy — decision-making authority improves performance and satisfaction.
- Recognition — appreciation reinforces positive behaviors.

**Key Inputs:** Resource management plan, issue log, lessons learned register, project schedule, resource calendars, team charter, work performance reports, project team assignments, team performance assessments.

**Key Tools & Techniques:** Colocation, virtual teams, communication technology, conflict management, influencing, motivation, negotiation, team building, decision-making, critical thinking, coaching and mentoring, training, problem-solving (Six Thinking Hats®), retrospectives, recognition and rewards, individual and team assessments, SWOT analysis, emotional intelligence, organizational cultural intelligence, leadership styles (distributed, centralized, servant leadership), Tuckman ladder, PMIS, virtual collaboration tools.

**Key Outputs:** Team performance assessments, change requests, project management plan updates (resource management plan, schedule baseline, cost baseline), project document updates (issue log, lessons learned register, project schedule, resource calendars, team charter, project team assignments).

### 5. Monitor and Control Resourcing

The process of ensuring that physical or virtual resources assigned and allocated to the project are available as planned, monitoring planned versus actual use, and performing corrective actions. Its key benefit is ensuring resources are available at the right time and place and released when no longer needed. Performed continuously throughout all project phases. (Team members are addressed in the Lead the Team process; this process is concerned with physical/virtual resources.)

**Key Activities:**
- Monitoring resource expenditures.
- Identifying and dealing with resource shortages/surpluses in a timely manner.
- Ensuring resources are used and released according to plan and project needs.
- Informing stakeholders of resource-related issues.
- Influencing factors that can create resource utilization changes.
- Managing actual changes as they occur.

**Key Inputs:** Resource management plan, issue log, lessons learned register, physical/virtual resource assignments, project schedule, resource breakdown structure, risk register, work performance data, agreements.

**Key Tools & Techniques:** Alternative analysis, cost-benefit analysis, performance reviews, trend analysis, problem-solving, negotiation, influencing, PMIS, value stream mapping, continuous improvement, theory of constraints, control charts, branch and bound.

**Key Outputs:** Work performance information, change requests, project management plan updates (resource management plan, schedule baseline, cost baseline), project document updates (assumption log, issue log, lessons learned register, physical resource assignments, RBS, risk register).

**Risks of Ineffective Resourcing Control:**
- Failing to secure critical equipment or infrastructure on time → manufacturing delays.
- Ordering low-quality materials → quality damage, high recalls/rework.
- Excessive inventory → high operational costs and reduced profit.
- Unacceptably low inventory → inability to satisfy customer demand.

## Tailoring Considerations

Given the uniqueness of each project, activities and processes within the Resources performance domain may be tailored. Key considerations include:

- **Development Approach Selection.** Leadership styles are tailored to project needs, environment, and stakeholders.
  - **Predictive approach:** Resource management is planned and structured in advance — define requirements early, allocate based on schedule, create detailed resource management plan, ensure availability throughout.
  - **Adaptive approach:** Projects with high variability benefit from self-organizing teams with generalizing specialists. Collaboration boosts productivity, accelerates integration of work activities, improves communication and knowledge sharing, and provides flexibility. Less time for centralized tasking and decision-making.

- **Maturity of Project Team Culture.** Cross-organizational teams (contracts, partnerships) may have more formalized, less flexible roles. Requires up-front work to establish a "one team" mindset and integrate skills, capabilities, and processes. Key dimensions:
  - **Unique team culture** — Each team develops its own culture through norms or informal behaviors, operating within the organization's culture.
  - **Transparency on bias** — Openness about biases (e.g., Gantt chart preferences, planning horizons) establishes a culture of trust enabling consensus and collaboration.
  - **Project manager role modeling** — Establishing a safe, respectful, nonjudgmental environment through:
    - *Transparency* — in thinking, choices, information processing, and biases.
    - *Integrity* — ethical behavior, honesty (sharing risks, assumptions, bad news early, accurate status).
    - *Respect* — for each person, their thinking, skills, perspective, and expertise.
    - *Positive discourse* — dialogue (resolving divergent opinions together) rather than debate (win-lose).
    - *Support* — problem-solving, removing impediments, encouragement, empathy, active listening.
    - *Courage* — suggesting new approaches, disagreeing with authority, trying new things.
    - *Celebrating success* — recognizing innovation, adaptation, service, learning, and "quick wins."

- **Industry Context.** Some industries have adopted lean management, just-in-time (JIT) manufacturing, Kaizen, total productive maintenance (TPM), theory of constraints (TOC), and other resource-related methods. Project managers should determine whether the performing organization has adopted such methods and adapt accordingly.

- **Organizational Governance Structures.** Projects operate within larger organizational systems. Top management's leadership style may be expected to be reflected in the team's leadership. Organizational structure influences the degree to which authority and accountability are centralized or distributed (see [[13_Tools_and_Techniques]]).

- **Sustainability Considerations.** Projects should consider environmental and social impact across the entire value chain: hiring, sourcing materials, transportation, disposal. This includes reducing emissions, using renewable energy, minimizing waste, and positively affecting local communities (see green human resource management in [[13_Tools_and_Techniques]]).

- **Maturity of Project Team Members.** Team members mature in their technical fields may need less oversight and direction than those new to the organization, team, or technical specialty.

- **Virtual Project Teams.** Global, distributed workforces face challenges in creating the same level of collaboration and relatedness as face-to-face work. Mitigation strategies include: collaboration sites, team sites for project information, audio/video conferencing, messaging/texting for ongoing contact, scheduling time to know remote members, at least one face-to-face meeting to establish relationships, and creating a team charter defining ways of working.

- **Emphasis on Employee Well-Being.** Addressing burnout and "quiet quitting" (minimal effort, reluctance to speak up, absenteeism). Companies are prioritizing mental health initiatives, work-life balance, flexible schedules, stress management programs, and generous time-off policies.

### Tailoring Examples

1. A large, high-variability project with an adaptive approach may find resource planning less predictable, requiring flexible agreements and lean methods to control costs and meet schedule demands.
2. A newly formed virtual team may encounter collaboration issues, addressed through collaboration sites, team knowledge bases, conferencing tools, ongoing messaging, relationship-building time, at least one face-to-face meeting, and a team charter.

## Interactions With Other Domains

The Resources performance domain interacts with all other performance domains. Key interactions include:

- **[[08_Stakeholders_Performance_Domain]], Resources, and [[10_Risk_Performance_Domain]]** — These three significantly impact project outcomes defined by schedule, cost, and scope parameters. Other projects may compete for the same available resources at the same time and location, considerably impacting costs, schedules, risks, scope, and quality.

- **[[06_Schedule_Performance_Domain]]** — The Estimate Resources process is closely coordinated with schedule estimation; resource availability directly influences activity durations and sequencing.

- **[[07_Finance_Performance_Domain]]** — The Estimate Resources process is closely coordinated with the Estimate Costs process to align resource estimation with the project's overall budget and financial constraints, ensuring financial viability and optimal resource utilization.

- **[[05_Scope_Performance_Domain]]** — Resource requirements derive from the scope baseline; scope changes trigger resource reassessment.

- **Integration across all domains** — Project managers lead the team to focus on what is essential at the project level through integration of processes, knowledge, and people. The project manager's main concern is leading the project team to achieve project objectives.

## Check Results

Activities in the Resources performance domain are considered successful only if they contribute to specific outcomes. The following table summarizes target outcomes and verification checks:

| Outcome | Check |
|---|---|
| **Shared ownership of the project** | All project team members know the vision and objectives. The team owns the deliverables and outcomes. |
| **High-performing team in place** | Team trusts one another and collaborates. Team adapts to changing situations and is resilient. Members feel empowered; the project manager empowers and recognizes team members while delivering results. |
| **Effective resource utilization** | Actual resource usage versus planned resource usage is effectively managed. |
| **Acceptable resource-downtime percentage** | Total downtime versus total planned resource time is at an acceptable level. |
| **Team productivity** | Units produced versus hours worked, or cost of work performed versus scheduled time, is at an acceptable level. KPIs are regularly reviewed to check team performance. |
| **Efficient management of physical/virtual resources** | Number of materials used, scrap discarded, and amount of rework indicate that resources are being used efficiently. |
| **Resource procurement management conducted** | Procurement processes, efficiency checks, order, manufacture, and delivery tracking are all successfully managed. |
