## Purpose

The Scope Performance Domain encompasses the processes necessary to define, develop, monitor, control, and verify the scope of a project, ensuring alignment with stakeholder expectations and project objectives. Its fundamental purpose is managing what is and is not included in the project — scope encapsulates a project's expected value, making it the most important component of any project's baseline.

## Key Concepts

### Product Scope vs. Project Scope

- ****Product Scope**** — A description of the features, functions, and characteristics of the product, service, or result the project is intended to deliver. It defines what the end result should look like and what it should do. The focus is on the deliverables themselves, including quality and performance specifications.
- ****Project Scope**** — Encompasses the work performed to deliver a product, service, or result with the specified features and functions. It ensures that the expected value is achieved. The delivery of scope should generate expected value that is worth the time and resources invested and ideally maximized.

### Scope Decomposition

- ****Work Breakdown Structure** (WBS)** — In predictive or hybrid approaches, a hierarchical decomposition of the total scope of work into smaller, manageable work packages that can be easily assigned, tracked, and measured. The WBS, together with the project scope statement and the WBS dictionary, constitutes the ****scope baseline****.
- ****WBS Dictionary**** — Provides additional details about each WBS component: scope descriptions, milestones, responsible parties, resource needs, and acceptance criteria. Used in projects with complex or interdependent deliverables.
- ****Value Breakdown Structure** (VBS)** — A hierarchical structure that connects project scope and its intended value to the product scope. Each deliverable is assigned a value-based number (e.g., dollars of revenue, lives saved) or percentage of expected total value, enabling prioritization and estimation of drag cost for critical path items.
- ****Product Backlog**** — In adaptive environments, a dynamic, prioritized list of work items and features. Decomposed into epics, features, and user stories. Provides a framework for managing scope by focusing on value-driven outcomes and continuous prioritization.

### Requirements Management

- ****Requirement**** — A condition or capability necessary in a product, service, or result to satisfy a business need.
- ****Definition of Done** (DoD)** — A checklist of criteria required for a deliverable to be considered ready for customer use. In adaptive approaches, it is a shared understanding of what must be met before a user story, feature, or increment is considered complete.
- ****Quality as a Feature**** — Quality is an integral attribute of scope, encompassing both functional and nonfunctional requirements. Scope includes target thresholds for attributes such as sturdiness, longevity, and maintainability.

### Scope Baseline

In predictive environments, the scope baseline is the approved version of formal scope documents — the project scope statement, WBS, and WBS dictionary — changeable only through formal change control procedures. It forms part of the ****performance measurement baseline**** (PMB) alongside the schedule baseline and cost baseline. In adaptive approaches, the baseline is defined at the start of each iteration and aligned with prioritized requirements, with the product owner dynamically approving changes without formal change control.

## Processes

### 1. Plan Scope Management

Creates a scope management plan that documents how the project and product scope will be defined, validated, and controlled. Defines all work required to complete the project and eliminates unnecessary work. In adaptive projects, the approach is more iterative and collaborative. The key benefit is providing guidance on scope management to ensure value delivery to stakeholders.

**Key inputs:** Project charter, project management plan, requirements documentation, risk register, stakeholder register.  
**Key outputs:** Scope management plan, requirements management plan.

### 2. Elicit and Analyze Requirements

Determines, documents, and manages stakeholder needs and requirements to meet project objectives. In adaptive environments, requirements are collected as user stories and prioritized in a backlog. The key benefit is providing direction and a starting point to define a product, service, or result that adds value.

**Key inputs:** Project charter, agreements, business case, assumption log, lessons learned register, stakeholder register.  
**Tools & techniques:** Benchmarking, brainstorming, focus groups, interviews, questionnaires and surveys, document analysis, nominal group technique, design thinking, prioritization/ranking.  
**Key outputs:** Requirements documentation.

### 3. Define Scope

Develops a detailed or high-level description of the project, product, and expected value to be delivered, along with a quality management plan. In predictive approaches, deliverables are structured in the WBS; in adaptive approaches, they are progressively defined through a backlog. Also identifies quality requirements and standards for deliverables and how compliance will be demonstrated.

**Key inputs:** Project charter, assumption log, requirements documentation.  
**Tools & techniques:** Expert judgment, decision-making, data analysis, decomposition, facilitation, product analysis.  
**Key outputs:** Project scope statement, requirements documentation updates.

### 4. Develop Scope Structure

Subdivides project deliverables and work into smaller, more manageable components. In predictive approaches, this produces the WBS, WBS dictionary, and scope baseline. In adaptive projects, this corresponds to product backlog decomposition into epics, features, and user stories. The key benefit is providing a strategic view of the project's scope and value, ensuring team alignment toward a common goal.

**Key inputs:** Project management plan, project scope statement, requirements documentation, approved changes.  
**Tools & techniques:** Expert judgment, brainstorming, decomposition.  
**Key outputs:** Scope baseline, WBS, WBS dictionary, user stories, product backlog.

### 5. Monitor and Control Scope

Ongoing process of monitoring the status of project and product scope, managing changes to the scope baseline, measuring the quality of deliverables, and ensuring fulfillment of required standards. Controls how change requests are processed while ensuring deliverables meet specified quality requirements and remain aligned to the scope baseline.

**Key inputs:** Scope management plan, quality management plan, requirements documentation, performance measurement baseline, quality metrics, approved change requests, deliverables.  
**Tools & techniques:** Variance analysis, trend analysis, root cause analysis, performance reviews, audits and inspections, testing/product evaluations, process automations.  
**Key outputs:** Quality reports, verified deliverables, change requests, quality control measurements, work performance information.

### 6. Validate Scope

Formalizes acceptance of the completed project deliverables. Has two main objectives: checking the processes used to achieve quality standards, and formalizing acceptance of deliverables by stakeholders. Increases the probability of acceptance through objective validation that assures value and quality.

**Key inputs:** Requirements documentation, scope baseline, quality reports, work performance data, quality control measurements, verified deliverables.  
**Tools & techniques:** Data gathering, data analysis, inspection, decision-making, customer talks and tests, process analysis, review meetings.  
**Key outputs:** Accepted deliverables, change requests, quality report updates, work performance information updates.

## Tailoring Considerations

Given the uniqueness of each project, scope activities and processes should be tailored:

- **Dependency on External Partners** — In projects involving third-party suppliers or subcontractors, scope must be integrated into contractual agreements early to set expectations and synchronize scope-related commitments across all parties.
- **Environmental Dynamics** — In high-dynamism environments (market volatility, rapid technological change, evolving customer preferences), scope management should be flexible. Iterative feedback mechanisms allow real-time scope adjustments to maintain relevance and high delivered value.
- **Design Phase Emphasis** — Industries like pharmaceuticals and construction rely heavily on the design phase. Significant effort should be invested in early scope definition to prevent costly changes later. Strategic rescoping during design allows comprehensive evaluation before substantial resource commitment.
- **Adaptive and Hybrid Life Cycles** — Adaptive approaches refine scope through multiple iterations. Hybrid projects present additional challenges: overall expectations are set with formal baselines (scope, schedule, cost), while subteams may work iteratively on backlog user stories — requiring tailored integration of both approaches.

### Examples

1. **New Product Development** — Using a backlog with user stories, the team focuses on main product goals rather than delivering all backlog items, refining scope through iterations.
2. **Mobile App in New Market** — Adopting an iterative approach with a clear minimum viable product (MVP) and minimal feature backlog provides rapid feedback and better adaptation to changes.
3. **City Park Construction** — With tight timelines and multiple stakeholders, the project manager invests more time in the design phase, creating multiple mock-ups before beginning construction.

## Interactions With Other Domains

Scope is deeply interconnected with other performance domains. Changes to scope ripple across the project:

- **[[06_Schedule_Performance_Domain]]** — Any scope change directly impacts the schedule; scope decomposition feeds schedule activity definition.
- **[[07_Finance_Performance_Domain]]** — Scope changes affect cost estimates and budget; the scope baseline, schedule baseline, and cost baseline together form the PMB.
- **[[10_Risk_Performance_Domain]]** — Managing risk is intrinsic to managing scope. Risks can alter a project's trajectory; rescoping may be employed as a strategy to mitigate, avoid, or transfer risks.
- **[[08_Stakeholders_Performance_Domain]]** — Stakeholder needs drive requirements; stakeholder acceptance validates scope deliverables.
- **[[09_Resources_Performance_Domain]]** — Scope changes may require additional resources, subject to governance review and approval processes.
- **[[04_Governance_Performance_Domain]]** — Scope changes must be navigated within established governance structures to ensure modifications are justified and aligned with strategic objectives of both the project and the organization.

## Check Results

The Scope performance domain is successful when the following outcomes are met:

| Outcome | Check |
|---------|-------|
| Effective change management | Predictive: change log demonstrating impact analysis across domains. Adaptive: backlog showing scope accomplishment rate, new scope addition rate, and stakeholder feedback incorporation. |
| Clear understanding of requirements | Predictive: fewer changes to initial requirements. Adaptive: each iteration provides clear short-term requirements; rolling wave approach refines longer-term requirements. |
| Alignment with business objectives and strategy | Business case, strategic plan, and authorizing documents demonstrate alignment between deliverables and business objectives. |
| Stakeholder satisfaction with deliverables | Interviews, observation, end-user feedback; complaint and return levels as satisfaction indicators. |
| Scope items clearly defined | Scope Definition Accuracy (%) = (Planned Scope Items Correctly Delivered / Total Planned Scope Items) × 100 |
| Scope creep measured | Scope Creep (%) = (Unplanned Deliverables / Total Deliverables) × 100 |
| Requirements stability measured | Requirements Stability (%) = (Unchanged Requirements / Total Requirements) × 100 |
| Sustainability considered | WBS or backlog includes activities for sustainability management (e.g., CO₂ emissions assessment, biodiversity impact management). |
