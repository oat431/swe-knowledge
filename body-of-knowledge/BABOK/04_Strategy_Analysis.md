# Strategy Analysis

> *Source: BABOK® Guide v3 by IIBA, Chapter 5 — Strategy Analysis*

## Purpose

Strategy Analysis defines the most effective way to apply the capabilities of an enterprise to reach a desired set of goals and objectives. Strategies may span the entire enterprise, a division, department, or region, and may involve changes to any aspect of the enterprise: organizational structure, culture, business processes, technology, infrastructure, or policies. When the outcome is predictable, a clear strategy can be planned out. When unpredictable, the strategy focuses more on mitigating risk, testing assumptions, and changing course until success is achieved — or until the initiative ends.

The Strategy Analysis knowledge area includes four tasks:

| Task | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Analyze Current State** | Understand the business need and how it relates to the way the enterprise functions today |
| **Define Future State** | Define goals and objectives that demonstrate the business need has been satisfied |
| **Assess Risks** | Understand uncertainties around the change and their effect on value delivery |
| **Define Change Strategy** | Perform gap analysis, assess options, and recommend the highest-value approach |

A strategy may be captured in a strategic plan, product vision, business case, product roadmap, or other artifacts.

## Analyze Current State (6.1)

### Purpose
Understand the reasons why an enterprise needs to change some aspect of how it operates and what would be directly or indirectly affected by the change.

### Description
Change is triggered by problems or opportunities that cannot be addressed without altering the current state. Business analysts help stakeholders enable change by exploring and articulating the business needs that drive the desire to change. The current state is explored in just enough detail to validate the need for a change and/or the change strategy.

The scope of the current state describes the important existing characteristics of the environment, bounded by the components of the enterprise and its environment as they relate to the needs. The current state can be described at different levels — from the entire enterprise to small components of a solution. It is rarely static while a change is being developed; internal and external influencers, as well as other organizational changes, can force alterations in the desired future state or change strategy.

### Inputs
- **Elicitation Results**: define and understand the current state
- **Needs**: the problem or opportunity that launches business analysis work

### Elements

#### .1 Business Needs
Problems and opportunities of strategic importance faced by the enterprise. They may be identified from:
- **Top-down**: a strategic goal that needs to be achieved
- **Bottom-up**: a problem with the current state of a process, function, or system
- **Middle management**: a manager needs additional information or must perform additional functions
- **External drivers**: customer demand or business competition in the marketplace

The way the need is defined determines which alternative solutions will be considered, which stakeholders will be consulted, and which solution approaches will be evaluated. Business analysts should question assumptions and constraints buried in the statement of the issue to ensure the correct problem is being solved.

Factors to consider: adverse impacts and their quantification, expected benefits, time-to-resolution, cost of doing nothing, and the underlying source of the problem.

#### .2 Organizational Structure and Culture
- **Organizational structure**: formal relationships between people; reporting structures may aid or limit a potential change
- **Organizational culture**: beliefs, values, and norms shared by members of the organization
- Cultural assessment identifies whether cultural changes are required, whether stakeholders understand the rationale for the current state, and whether stakeholders view the current state as satisfactory

#### .3 Capabilities and Processes
The activities an enterprise performs, including knowledge, products/services, functions, and decision-making methods.

- **Capability-centric view**: useful for innovative solutions that combine existing capabilities to produce new outcomes (organized in a functional hierarchy)
- **Process-centric view**: useful for improving performance of current activities (organized end-to-end across the enterprise)

Core capabilities/processes are measured by performance indicators that can assess the benefits of a change.

#### .4 Technology and Infrastructure
Information systems that support people in executing processes, making decisions, and interacting with suppliers and customers. Infrastructure includes computer hardware, physical plants, logistics, and their operation and upkeep.

#### .5 Policies
Define the scope of decision making at different levels. They address routine operations, ensure correct decisions, provide guidance on permitted behavior, support governance, and determine how new resources can be acquired. Relevant policies may shape the solution space and may be constraints on possible actions.

#### .6 Business Architecture
No part of the current state should be assessed in complete isolation. Business analysts must understand how all elements fit together and support one another. If existing needs are not recognized or continue to be unmet by a transition or future state, changes are likely to result in a loss of value.

#### .7 Internal Assets
Tangible and intangible resources (financial resources, patents, reputation, brand names) used in the current state.

#### .8 External Influencers
Sources that do not participate in a change but present constraints, dependencies, or drivers:
- **Industry Structure**: distinct value-creation patterns within an industry
- **Competitors**: nature and intensity of competition; entry of new competitors
- **Customers**: size and nature of customer segments, negotiating power, price sensitivity
- **Suppliers**: variety, diversity, and power over customers
- **Political and Regulatory Environment**: current and potential impact of laws and regulations
- **Technology**: productivity-enhancing potential of recent and expected innovations
- **Macroeconomic Factors**: trade, unemployment, inflation, and other economic conditions

### Guidelines and Tools
- **Business Analysis Approach**: guides how the business analyst undertakes current state analysis
- **Enterprise Limitation**: understanding challenges that exist within the enterprise
- **Organizational Strategy**: goals and objectives that guide operations and provide vision for the future state
- **Solution Limitation**: understanding the current state and challenges of existing solutions
- **Solution Performance Goals**: measure current performance as a baseline for setting future state goals
- **Solution Performance Measures**: describe actual performance of existing solutions
- **Stakeholder Analysis Results**: stakeholders contribute to understanding and analysis

### Techniques
- Benchmarking and Market Analysis (5 Forces, PEST, STEEP, CATWOE)
- Business Capability Analysis
- Business Model Canvas
- Business Cases
- Concept Modelling
- Data Mining
- Document Analysis
- Financial Analysis
- Focus Groups
- Functional Decomposition
- Interviews
- Item Tracking
- Lessons Learned
- Metrics and Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)
- Mind Mapping
- Observation
- Organizational Modelling
- Process Analysis
- Process Modelling
- Risk Analysis and Management
- Root Cause Analysis
- Scope Modelling
- Survey or Questionnaire
- SWOT Analysis
- Vendor Assessment
- Workshops

### Stakeholders
Customer, Domain Subject Matter Expert, End User, Implementation Subject Matter Expert, Operational Support, Project Manager, Regulator, Sponsor, Supplier, Tester

### Outputs
- **Current State Description**: context of the enterprise's scope, capabilities, resources, performance, culture, dependencies, infrastructure, external influences, and significant relationships between these elements
- **Business Requirements**: the problem, opportunity, or constraint defined based on an understanding of the current state

## Define Future State (6.2)

### Purpose
Determine the set of necessary conditions to meet the business need.

### Description
All purposeful change must include a definition of success. The future state is defined at a level of detail that:
- Allows for competing strategies to achieve the future state to be identified and assessed
- Provides a clear definition of the outcomes that will satisfy the business needs
- Details the scope of the solution space
- Allows for value associated with the future state to be assessed
- Enables consensus to be achieved among key stakeholders

The future state description includes the new, removed, and modified components of the enterprise. Change may be needed to: business processes, functions, lines of business, organization structures, staff competencies, knowledge and skills, training, facilities, desktop tools, organization locations, data and information, application systems, and technology infrastructure.

In predictable environments, the purpose is to gather sufficient information to make the best choices among potential options. In uncertain environments, the future state may be defined by identification of appropriate performance measures, and the change strategy will support exploration of multiple options.

### Inputs
- **Business Requirements**: the problems, opportunities, or constraints the future state will address

### Elements

#### .1 Business Goals and Objectives
- **Goals**: longer-term, ongoing, qualitative statements of a state or condition the organization is seeking to establish and maintain (e.g., increase customer satisfaction, comply with new regulations, reduce time to deliver)
- **Objectives**: more descriptive, granular, specific, and linked to measures that make it possible to objectively assess if the objective has been achieved
- **SMART test**: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bounded

#### .2 Scope of Solution Space
Defines which kinds of options will be considered when investigating possible solutions, including changes to organizational structure/culture, capabilities/processes, technology/infrastructure, policies, products/services, or external relationships. Critical considerations include quantitative/qualitative value, time needed, and opportunity cost.

#### .3 Constraints
Aspects of the current state, planned future state that may not be changed, or mandatory design elements. May reflect: budgetary restrictions, time restrictions, technology, infrastructure, policies, resource limits, skill restrictions, stakeholder non-impact requirements, regulatory compliance, or any other restriction. Must be carefully examined for accuracy and justification.

#### .4–.9 Organizational Structure & Culture, Capabilities & Processes, Technology & Infrastructure, Policies, Business Architecture, Internal Assets
Describes the desired state of each component — what needs to change, what new resources or capabilities are needed, what technical constraints exist, how policies may open or restrict options, and how all elements must effectively support one another and contribute to meeting business goals.

#### .10 Identify Assumptions
Most strategies are predicated on assumptions that determine whether the strategy can succeed, particularly in uncertain environments. Assumptions must be identified and clearly understood so appropriate decisions can be made if they later prove invalid. Change strategies can be structured to test assumptions as early as possible.

#### .11 Potential Value
The net benefit of the solution after operating costs are accounted for. A change must result in greater value than if no action was taken. Business analysts consider increased or decreased potential value from: external opportunities, new partners, new technologies/knowledge, loss of competitors, and mandated adoption. The potential value is a key component of the business case. The analysis should also consider the acceptable level of investment to reach the future state.

### Guidelines and Tools
- **Current State Description**: context and starting point for the future state
- **Metrics and Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)**: determine whether the desired future state has been achieved
- **Organizational Strategy**: path, method, or approach to achieve the desired future state

### Techniques
Acceptance and Evaluation Criteria, Balanced Scorecard, Benchmarking and Market Analysis, Brainstorming, Business Capability Analysis, Business Cases, Business Model Canvas, Decision Analysis, Decision Modelling, Financial Analysis, Functional Decomposition, Interviews, Lessons Learned, Metrics and KPIs, Mind Mapping, Organizational Modelling, Process Modelling, Prototyping, Scope Modelling, Survey or Questionnaire, SWOT Analysis, Vendor Assessment, Workshops

### Stakeholders
Customer, Domain Subject Matter Expert, End User, Implementation Subject Matter Expert, Operational Support, Project Manager, Regulator, Sponsor, Supplier, Tester

### Outputs
- **Business Objectives**: the desired direction the business wishes to pursue to achieve the future state
- **Future State Description**: boundaries of the proposed new, removed, and modified components of the enterprise and the potential value expected
- **Potential Value**: the value that may be realized by implementing the proposed future state

## Assess Risks (6.3)

### Purpose
Understand the undesirable consequences of internal and external forces on the enterprise during a transition to, or once in, the future state. An understanding of the potential impact of those forces can be used to make a recommendation about a course of action.

### Description
Risk assessment includes analyzing and managing risks related to the current state, a desired future state, a change itself, a change strategy, or any tasks being performed by the enterprise. Risks are analyzed for:
- Possible consequences if the risk occurs
- Impact of those consequences
- Likelihood of the risk
- Potential time frame when the risk might occur

The collection of risks is used as input for selecting or coordinating a change strategy. A risk assessment can include choosing to accept a risk if the effort required to modify the risk or the level of risk outweighs the probable loss.

> **Note:** The BABOK® Guide treats "risk" as uncertain events that can produce *negative* outcomes. "Opportunities" are captured as needs and managed accordingly.

### Inputs
- **Business Objectives**: describing the desired direction to identify and discuss potential risks
- **Elicitation Results (confirmed)**: stakeholder perceptions of risks to realizing the desired future state
- **Influences**: internal and external factors that will impact the realization of the desired future state
- **Potential Value**: a benchmark against which risks can be assessed
- **Requirements (prioritized)**: depending on priority, influence the risks to be defined and understood

### Elements

#### .1 Unknowns
When assessing risk, there will be uncertainty in likelihood and impact. Business analysts collaborate with stakeholders to assess risks based on current understanding. Historical contexts from similar situations and lessons learned from past changes assist in deciding the impact and likelihood of risks.

#### .2 Constraints, Assumptions, and Dependencies
These can be analyzed for risks and sometimes should be managed as risks themselves. If related to an aspect of a change, they can be restated as a risk by identifying the event or condition and consequences that could occur.

#### .3 Negative Impact to Value
Risks are expressed as conditions that increase the likelihood or severity of a negative impact to value. Business analysts clearly identify and express each risk and estimate its likelihood and impact to determine the level of risk. A total risk level can be estimated from the aggregated set of risks — in some cases quantified in financial terms, time, effort, or other measures.

#### .4 Risk Tolerance
How much uncertainty a stakeholder or enterprise is willing to take on in exchange for potential value:
- **Risk-aversion**: unwillingness to accept much uncertainty; preference to avoid high-risk courses or invest more to reduce risks
- **Neutrality**: some level of risk is acceptable, provided the course of action does not result in a loss
- **Risk-seeking**: willingness to accept or take on more risk in return for higher potential value

An individual or organization may exhibit different risk tolerances at different times. Typically, the highest-level risks are dealt with regardless of the risk tolerance level.

#### .5 Recommendation
Based on risk analysis, business analysts recommend a course of action:
- Pursue the benefits of a change regardless of the risk
- Pursue the benefits while investing in reducing risk (likelihood and/or impact)
- Seek ways to increase benefits to outweigh the risk
- Identify ways to manage and optimize opportunities
- Do not pursue the benefits of a change

If the change proceeds with risks, stakeholders should be identified to monitor risks and consequences. A plan of action may be developed before the risk materializes.

### Guidelines and Tools
- **Business Analysis Approach**: guides how the business analyst analyzes risks
- **Business Policies**: define the limits within which decisions must be made; may mandate or govern risk management
- **Change Strategy**: provides the plan to transition; must be assessed to understand associated risks
- **Current State Description**: determines risks associated with the current state
- **Future State Description**: determines risks associated with the future state
- **Identified Risks**: starting point for more thorough risk assessment (from prior Risk Analysis Results, elicitation, or expert opinion)
- **Stakeholder Engagement Approach**: helps identify and assess potential impact of internal and external forces

### Techniques
Brainstorming, Business Cases, Decision Analysis, Document Analysis, Financial Analysis, Interviews, Lessons Learned, Mind Mapping, Risk Analysis and Management, Root Cause Analysis, Survey or Questionnaire, Workshops

### Stakeholders
Domain Subject Matter Expert, Implementation Subject Matter Expert, Operational Support, Project Manager, Regulator, Sponsor, Supplier, Tester

### Outputs
- **Risk Analysis Results**: understanding of the risks associated with achieving the future state, and the mitigation strategies which will be used to prevent risks, reduce the impact, or reduce the likelihood of the risk occurring

## Define Change Strategy (6.4)

### Purpose
Develop and assess alternative approaches to the change, and then select the recommended approach.

### Description
The change strategy clearly describes the nature of the change in terms of:
- Context of the change
- Identified alternative change strategies
- Justification for why a particular change strategy is the best approach
- Investment and resources required to work toward the future state
- How the enterprise will realize value after the solution is delivered
- Key stakeholders in the change
- Transition states along the way

The change strategy might be presented as part of a business case, Statement of Work (SOW), an enterprise's strategic plan, or in other formats. Change strategies can entail attaining only parts of a future state initially. For each transition state, the strategy should clarify which parts of the solution are completed, which are not, and which parts of the value can be realized.

### Inputs
- **Current State Description**: context about the current state, including internal and external influences
- **Future State Description**: context about the desired future state
- **Risk Analysis Results**: identified risks and exposure of each risk
- **Stakeholder Engagement Approach**: understanding stakeholders' communication and collaboration needs

### Elements

#### .1 Solution Scope
The outcome of a change that allows an enterprise to satisfy a need. Multiple solution options might be evaluated, and the best approach is justified and selected. The solution scope defines the boundaries of the solution, described in enough detail to enable stakeholders to understand which new capabilities the change will deliver and how the proposed solution enables the future state's goals.

Can be described in terms of: capabilities, technology, business rules, business decisions, data, processes, resources, knowledge and skills, models/descriptions of markets, functions, locations, networks, organizational structures, workflows, events, sequence, motivations, or business logic. Can also include out-of-scope components for clarity.

#### .2 Gap Analysis
Identifies the difference between current state and future state capabilities. Using the same techniques to describe both states simplifies the comparison. Capabilities analyzed can include: processes, functions, lines of business, organizational structures, locations, data and information, staff competencies, knowledge and skills, training, facilities, application systems, and technology infrastructure.

Gap analysis can determine if the enterprise can meet its needs using its existing structure, resources, capabilities, and technology. If yes, the change will likely be small — or there may be no change at all.

#### .3 Enterprise Readiness Assessment
Analyzes the enterprise to assess its capacity to:
- Make the change and sustain it in the future state
- Use and sustain the solution and realize value from it

Factors considered: cultural readiness, operational readiness, timeline from implementation to value realization, and resources available to support the change effort.

#### .4 Change Strategy
A high-level plan of key activities and events used to transform the enterprise from current state to future state. May be a singular initiative composed of smaller changes structured as a set or sequence of projects, or as continuous improvement efforts.

During development, several options are identified, explored, and described in enough detail to determine feasibility. Sources of ideas include historical ideas, historical changes, other markets' strategies, and competitors' approaches. A preferred change strategy is selected considering:
- Organizational readiness to make the change
- Major costs and investments needed
- Timelines to make the change
- Alignment to business objectives
- Timelines for value realization
- Opportunity costs of the change strategy

Business analysts may develop a business case for each potential strategy. The net benefits of a future state may be very high, but if the investment is unbearable the enterprise may pass on the opportunity.

#### .5 Transition States and Release Planning
In many cases, the future state will need to be achieved over time through multiple transition states. Release planning determines which requirements to include in each release, phase, or iteration. Factors guiding these decisions include: overall budget, deadlines, resource constraints, training schedules, ability of the business to absorb changes, and organizational restraints or policies. Business analysts assist in planning the timing to cause minimal disruption and ensure all parties understand the impact.

### Guidelines and Tools
- **Business Analysis Approach**: guides how the business analyst defines a change strategy
- **Design Options**: various ways to satisfy business needs; each comes with its own change challenges
- **Solution Recommendations**: possible solutions identified by various SMEs to achieve the future state

### Techniques
Balanced Scorecard, Benchmarking and Market Analysis, Brainstorming, Business Capability Analysis, Business Cases, Business Model Canvas, Decision Analysis, Estimation, Financial Analysis, Focus Groups, Functional Decomposition, Interviews, Lessons Learned, Mind Mapping, Organizational Modelling, Process Modelling, Scope Modelling, SWOT Analysis, Vendor Assessment, Workshops

### Stakeholders
Customer, Domain Subject Matter Expert, End User, Implementation Subject Matter Expert, Operational Support, Project Manager, Regulator, Sponsor, Supplier, Tester

### Outputs
- **Change Strategy**: the approach that the organization will follow to guide change
- **Solution Scope**: the solution scope that will be achieved through execution of the change strategy
