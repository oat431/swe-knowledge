# Introduction to SWEBOK v4

> **Purpose:** The SWEBOK Guide, maintained by the IEEE Computer Society's Professional and Educational Activities Board, defines the generally accepted knowledge of the software engineering discipline. Version 4 updates the 2014 V3 edition to improve currency, readability, consistency, and usability, incorporating modern practices like Agile, DevOps, AI/ML, and IoT across its knowledge areas.

## What Is the SWEBOK Guide?

The SWEBOK Guide is **not** the body of knowledge itself—that exists in published literature. Rather, the Guide describes, organizes, and provides topical access to the *generally accepted* portion of that body of knowledge. It serves five explicit objectives:

1. **Promote a consistent worldwide view** of software engineering
2. **Specify the scope** of software engineering and clarify its place relative to other disciplines
3. **Characterize the contents** of the discipline
4. **Provide topical access** to the body of knowledge
5. **Provide a foundation** for curriculum development, certification, and licensing

The V4 development process engaged over 130 reviewers from the Americas, Asia, Europe, and Oceania, coordinated through associate editors and public presentations at international venues.

## Structure of the Guide

The Guide comprises **18 Knowledge Areas (KAs)** followed by three appendixes. A KA is an identified area of software engineering described in terms of its generally accepted knowledge—processes, practices, inputs, outputs, tools, and techniques.

The 18 KAs divide into two groups:

- **Chapters 1–15: Software engineering KAs** — covering the practice and engineering economics of the discipline, from requirements through maintenance, security, and professional practice.
- **Chapters 16–18: Foundation KAs** — Computing Foundations, Mathematical Foundations, and Engineering Foundations, which distill the relevant theory from computer science and engineering.

Each KA uses a **two- or three-level hierarchical breakdown** into labeled topics and subtopics, compatible with major schools of thought and industry practice. Breakdowns are not designed for particular application domains, business models, or development methodologies; they describe what is generally found across the discipline.

Three appendixes support the KAs:
- **Appendix A:** KA description specifications
- **Appendix B:** Annotated standards relevant to each KA
- **Appendix C:** Consolidated reference list

Each KA chapter also includes a **matrix** connecting reference materials to the topics covered.

## What's New in V4

Since V3, all KAs have been updated to reflect changes in the field:
- **Agile and DevOps** are incorporated into nearly all KAs
- **Three new KAs** were added: Software Architecture, Software Security, and Software Engineering Operations
- **AI, ML, and IoT** are integrated into the foundation KAs
- References to **system types** (enterprise/cloud, embedded/IoT, AI/ML-based systems) appear where practices diverge

## Key Definitions

- **Software** (SEVOCAB): "Computer programs, procedures and possibly associated documentation and data pertaining to the operation of a computer system."

- **Software Engineering** (SEVOCAB): "The application of a systematic, disciplined, quantifiable approach to the development, operation, and maintenance of software; that is, the application of engineering to software."

- **Software Engineering vs. Computer Science**: Science discovers new things; engineering applies knowledge to solve real-world problems cost-effectively. Sending a computer scientist to solve a software engineering problem makes as little sense as sending a chemist to solve a chemical engineering problem.

- **Generally Accepted Knowledge**: Knowledge applicable to most projects most of the time, with consensus about its value and usefulness. It is distinct from *advanced/research knowledge* (too immature) and *specialized knowledge* (too narrow). Selection of what to apply depends on each project's needs.

- **Knowledge Area (KA)**: An identified area of software engineering described in terms of its generally accepted component processes, practices, inputs, outputs, tools, and techniques.

## Related Disciplines

The Guide recognizes **eleven related disciplines** that intersect with software engineering but whose knowledge is not characterized within the SWEBOK Guide itself:

Business Analysis · Computer Engineering · Computer Science · Cybersecurity · Data Science · General Management · Information Systems and Technology · Mathematics · Project Management · Quality Management · Systems Engineering
