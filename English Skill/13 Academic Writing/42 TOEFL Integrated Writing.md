# TOEFL Integrated Writing

TOEFL Writing Task 1 is **integrated writing** — you read a passage (3 min), listen to a lecture (2 min), then write a summary comparing them (20 min, 150-225 words).

The lecture **always challenges** the reading passage.

---

## The Format

| Phase | Time | What Happens |
|-------|------|-------------|
| **Reading** | 3 minutes | A short passage (~250 words) presents 3 points |
| **Listening** | ~2 minutes | A lecture challenges each of the 3 points |
| **Writing** | 20 minutes | Summarize how the lecture challenges the reading |

> **You can see the reading passage while writing.** You CANNOT replay the lecture.

---

## Note-Taking Template

Use this grid while listening:

```
┌──────────────┬──────────────────┬──────────────────┐
│   POINT      │  READING SAYS    │  LECTURE SAYS    │
├──────────────┼──────────────────┼──────────────────┤
│  Point 1     │                  │                  │
├──────────────┼──────────────────┼──────────────────┤
│  Point 2     │                  │                  │
├──────────────┼──────────────────┼──────────────────┤
│  Point 3     │                  │                  │
└──────────────┴──────────────────┴──────────────────┘
```

Fill in the Reading column during the 3-minute reading period. Fill in the Lecture column while listening.

---

## Essay Structure

### Template

| Paragraph | Content | Words |
|-----------|---------|-------|
| **1. Introduction** | The reading and lecture discuss [topic]. The reading claims [3 points]. However, the lecturer challenges each of these points. | ~40 |
| **2. Point 1** | First, the reading states that [Point 1]. The lecturer refutes this by explaining that [counter-argument]. | ~50 |
| **3. Point 2** | Second, the reading argues that [Point 2]. However, the lecturer counters this by pointing out that [counter-argument]. | ~50 |
| **4. Point 3** | Finally, the reading claims that [Point 3]. The lecturer disputes this by noting that [counter-argument]. | ~50 |

### Example

> **Reading:** Solar energy is the best renewable energy source because (1) it's abundant, (2) technology is improving, and (3) costs are declining.

> **Lecture:** Challenges all three points.

**Model Response:**

> "The reading passage and the lecture both discuss solar energy as a renewable energy source. While the reading argues that solar energy is the best option, the lecturer challenges each of the reading's claims.
>
> First, the reading states that solar energy is abundant because the sun provides unlimited energy. However, the lecturer refutes this by pointing out that solar panels can only generate electricity during daylight hours and are ineffective in cloudy regions, making the energy supply inconsistent.
>
> Second, the reading argues that solar technology is rapidly improving. The lecturer counters this by explaining that current battery storage technology is insufficient to store large amounts of energy, meaning excess solar power generated during the day cannot be saved for nighttime use.
>
> Finally, the reading claims that solar energy costs are declining. The lecturer disputes this by noting that while panel costs have decreased, the total cost of installation, maintenance, and grid connection remains high, making solar energy more expensive than fossil fuels in many areas."

## Useful Language for Integrated Writing

### Introducing the Reading

| Phrase | Use |
|--------|-----|
| "The reading passage states that..." | Introducing a claim |
| "According to the reading..." | Citing the passage |
| "The author argues that..." | Presenting the argument |
| "The reading claims that..." | Another way to introduce a point |

### Introducing the Lecture

| Phrase | Use |
|--------|-----|
| "The lecturer challenges this by..." | Countering |
| "The lecturer refutes this argument by..." | Stronger counter |
| "However, the professor counters that..." | Contrasting |
| "The lecturer disputes this claim by..." | Disagreeing |
| "According to the lecturer,..." | Citing the lecture |
| "The professor points out that..." | Presenting evidence |

### Connecting Reading and Lecture

| Phrase | Use |
|--------|-----|
| "In contrast," | Simple contrast |
| "On the other hand," | Alternative perspective |
| "However," | General contrast |
| "The lecturer disagrees, arguing that..." | Direct contradiction |

## Key Rules

| Rule | Why |
|------|-----|
| **Don't give your opinion** | You're summarizing, not arguing |
| **Focus on the LECTURE** | The lecture is more important than the reading |
| **Use specific details** | Don't be vague — include the lecturer's exact examples |
| **Don't copy the reading** | Paraphrase — use your own words |
| **Stay within 150-225 words** | Too short = missing info. Too long = time waste. |

---

## What the Graders Look For

| Criterion | What It Means |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Content** | Did you accurately represent both the reading and the lecture? |
| **Organization** | Is your essay structured clearly (intro + 3 body paragraphs)? |
| **Language** | Is your grammar and vocabulary appropriate? |

---

## ⚠️ Thai Speaker Traps

| Trap | Problem | Fix |
|------|---------|-----|
| Giving your own opinion | "I think solar energy is good." | Never give your opinion — just report what the reading and lecture say |
| Focusing on the reading | Writing mostly about the reading | The lecture is more important — give it more space |
| Not taking notes | Forgetting the lecture details | Use the grid template while listening |
| Missing a point | Only discussing 2 of 3 points | Always address all 3 points |
| Copying from the reading | Penalized for not paraphrasing | Change the words and sentence structure |
