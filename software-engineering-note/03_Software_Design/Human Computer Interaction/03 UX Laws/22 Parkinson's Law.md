# 22 Parkinson's Law

> **Work expands to fill the time available for its completion.**

In UX: a task will take as long as you give the user to complete it. If you give them a massive textarea, they'll write a novel. If you give them a compact form, they'll fill it out faster. Constraints improve completion rates.

---

## The Principle

Give users **less space and fewer options** and they'll complete tasks faster with less overthinking.

---

## In Practice

### Form Design

| ❌ Expansive (Slow) | ✅ Constrained (Fast) |
|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Huge textarea for "Reason for return" | "Select reason" dropdown + optional short note |
| Open-ended date fields ("Enter date") | Date picker with today pre-selected |
| Free-text address field | Structured fields + autocomplete |

### Content Creation

| ❌ | ✅ |
|----|-----|
| Empty canvas — "Write your post..." (paralysis) | Template with prompts: "Title", "What did you learn?", "Key takeaways" |
| No character limit — users ramble | Character counter with recommended range |

### Time-Boxing

| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| Limited-time offers | "Sale ends in 2 hours" — Parkinson's + scarcity |
| Multi-step checkout | Progress indicator: 3 short steps vs 1 long form — same inputs, faster completion |
| Meeting scheduling | 30-min default slot vs open-ended — shorter meetings |

---

## The Counter-Principle

Some tasks need space — creative work, writing, design. Don't over-constrain when the goal is quality over speed.

| Apply Parkinson's Law | Don't Apply |
|----------------------|-------------|
| Checkout flows, signups, surveys | Writing tools, design canvases, code editors |
| Where completion speed matters | Where quality depth matters |
