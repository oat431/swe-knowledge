# 20 Serial Position Effect

> **Users remember the first and last items in a series best.**

The beginning (primacy effect) and end (recency effect) of any list are more memorable than the middle. This shapes how you should order navigation items, pricing tiers, and content.

## In Practice

### Navigation & Menus

| ❌ | ✅ |
|----|-----|
| Most important item in the middle of the nav | Most important items at the **left edge** (primacy) or **right edge** (recency) |
| Random order of items | Put critical actions where users will remember them |

### Pricing Tables

| Position | Strategy |
|----------|----------|
| **Left (first)** | Cheapest tier — anchors the price and makes others look reasonable |
| **Center** | Your recommended plan — badge it "Most Popular" |
| **Right (last)** | Enterprise/highest tier — recency makes it memorable for high-value users |

### Lists & Content

| Context | Apply Serial Position |
|---------|----------------------|
| Feature lists | Lead with strongest feature, end with second-strongest |
| Onboarding steps | First step = easiest win. Last step = celebration/summary. |
| Product comparisons | Key differentiator first, second-best last |

---

## The Middle Problem

The middle gets forgotten. Strategies to fix this:

| Strategy | Example |
|----------|---------|
| **Chunk it** | Break long lists into sections — each section has its own primacy/recency |
| **Highlight one** | "Most Popular" badge on the middle pricing tier |
| **Shorten the list** | 3–5 items instead of 10 |
