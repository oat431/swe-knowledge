# Software Engineering Professional Practice — Overview

> **Source:** [[SWEBOK v4 - Overview|SWEBOK v4]] Chapter 14 — Software Engineering Professional Practice
> **Purpose:** Professionalism, ethics, group dynamics, psychology, and communication skills — the non-technical foundations of responsible, accountable engineering practice.

## What Is This?

Software Engineering Professional Practice covers the knowledge, skills, and attitudes that make a software engineer a *professional* — not just a technically competent coder. It addresses the ethical obligations, interpersonal dynamics, communication requirements, and psychological awareness that separate engineering from hacking.

This is the KA that asks: *Should we build this?* and *How do we work together effectively?* — questions that technical skills alone can't answer. Every decision a software engineer makes has consequences for users, employers, society, and the profession. Professional Practice provides the framework for navigating those responsibilities.

The topics here aren't optional soft skills — they're foundational competencies. An engineer who can't communicate a design decision, who ignores ethical implications of an algorithm, or who can't collaborate effectively is a liability regardless of technical brilliance.

## The 6 Topic Areas

### 1. [[01_Professionalism]]

- What it means to be a professional software engineer
- Professional identity and the engineering mindset
- Commitment to quality, integrity, and continuous improvement
- Professional societies (IEEE, ACM) and their codes of ethics
- Licensing and certification (PE, CSDP)
- **Book:** *The Clean Coder: A Code of Conduct for Professional Programmers* (2011) — Robert C. Martin

### 2. [[02_Ethics]]

- The ACM/IEEE Code of Ethics for Software Engineers
- Ethical frameworks: utilitarianism, deontology, virtue ethics applied to SE
- Whistleblowing and ethical dissent
- Privacy, surveillance, and data ethics (GDPR, CCPA)
- Algorithmic bias and fairness
- AI ethics and responsible AI development
- **Book:** *Ethics in Computing* (2020) — Joseph Migga Kizza
- **Book:** *The Ethical Algorithm* (2019) — Michael Kearns & Aaron Roth

### 3. [[03_Group_Dynamics]]

- Team formation and development (Tuckman's model: forming → storming → norming → performing)
- Roles and responsibilities in software teams
- Conflict resolution and negotiation
- Collaboration models: pair programming, mob programming, code review
- Remote and distributed team dynamics
- **Book:** *Peopleware: Productive Projects and Teams, 3rd Ed.* (2013) — Tom DeMarco & Timothy Lister
- **Book:** *The Five Dysfunctions of a Team* (2002) — Patrick Lencioni

### 4. [[04_Psychology_of_Software_Engineering]]

- Cognitive biases in software development (confirmation bias, anchoring, Dunning-Kruger)
- Flow state and deep work
- Motivation: autonomy, mastery, purpose (Daniel Pink)
- Technical debt as a psychological phenomenon
- Burnout and sustainable pace
- Growth mindset vs. fixed mindset
- **Book:** *The Psychology of Computer Programming, Silver Anniversary Ed.* (1998) — Gerald Weinberg

### 5. [[05_Communication_Skills]]

- Technical writing: documentation, design documents, RFCs, ADRs
- Presenting technical content to non-technical stakeholders
- Active listening and requirements elicitation
- Code review as communication
- Writing effective emails, Slack messages, and meeting notes
- Visual communication: diagrams, whiteboards, architecture sketches
- **Book:** *Docs for Developers* (2021) — Bhatti, Corleissen, Lambourne, Nunez & Waterhouse

### 6. [[06_Intellectual_Property]]

- Copyright, patents, trade secrets, and trademarks in software
- Open source licenses (MIT, Apache 2.0, GPL, LGPL, AGPL)
- License compatibility and compliance
- Contributing to open source projects
- IP clauses in employment contracts
- **Book:** *Open Source Licensing: Software Freedom and Intellectual Property Law* (2004) — Lawrence Rosen
