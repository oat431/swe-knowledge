# 02 The Bill of Rights

Agile is supposed to heal the divide between business and development. Both sides have complementary **rights** — not just responsibilities.

---

## Customer Rights

| Right | What It Means |
|-------|--------------|
| **An overall plan** | What can be accomplished, when, at what cost? |
| **Get the most out of every iteration** | Every sprint delivers maximum value |
| **See progress in passing tests** | They define the tests. Green tests = real progress. |
| **Change their minds and priorities** | Requirements evolve. The process must allow it. |
| **Be informed on schedule and estimate changes** | No surprises. Transparent communication. |
| **Cancel at any time and remain with a working system** | Even if cancelled, the system still works and has value. |

---

## Developer Rights

| Right | What It Means |
|-------|--------------|
| **Know what is needed and what the priorities are** | Clear requirements. No guessing. |
| **Produce high-quality work** | Developers have the right to do their job well. Rushing = denied. |
| **Ask for and receive help** | No lone wolves. Team support is a right. |
| **Update their estimates** | As knowledge grows, estimates change. Honesty must be accepted. |
| **Accept responsibilities rather than having them assigned** | Autonomy. Developers choose their work. |

---

## What This Means

> Agile is not a process. It is a set of **rights, expectations, and disciplines** that form the basis for an ethical profession.

When either side violates these rights:
- Customers who refuse to prioritize → development can't deliver maximum value
- Managers who assign stories → developers lose autonomy and ownership
- Developers who give fake estimates → trust erodes, plans fail

The Bill of Rights is the social contract of Agile. Without it, Agile is just another top-down process.
