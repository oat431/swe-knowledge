# 04 Sustainable Pace

A software project is a **marathon**, not a sprint (despite Scrum's terminology). Working long hours isn't dedication — it's a consequence of bad planning.

---

## The Overtime Trap

| The Feeling | The Reality |
|------------|------------|
| "I'm valuable and needed" | Burnout with long-term damage to programmer and employer |
| "We saved the project with overtime" | The project was badly planned from the start |
| "Working late shows dedication" | It shows manipulation — developers coerced into unrealistic deadlines |

### Why Late-Night Work is Dangerous

> Judgment is impeded when working late at night after a full day. Grave mistakes and bad decisions are made at that point.

The code written at 2am is the code you'll be debugging at 10am — if you even remember writing it.

---

## Marathon, Not Sprint

| Marathon Mindset | Sprint Mindset |
|-----------------|---------------|
| Steady pace, sustainable | Go all-out, collapse |
| Finish the race | Finish this sprint and die |
| Energy conserved for the long haul | Burnout inevitable |

> If there's spare energy just before the finish line, it's OK to sprint for that last stretch. But you can't sprint the whole marathon.

---

## Sleep Is Non-Negotiable

> Programmers should figure out how many hours of sleep they need, and make it a priority to consistently get that amount of sleep.

Sleep-deprived programmers:
- Make more errors
- Write lower-quality code
- Make worse design decisions
- Are more irritable with teammates

---

## When Management Says "Go Faster"

| ❌ Don't | ✅ Do |
|----------|------|
| Comply and work overtime | Explain why the pace is unsustainable |
| Prove your dedication through exhaustion | Prove your dedication through consistent quality |
| Accept unrealistic deadlines | Provide honest estimates (ranges, not exact numbers) |

> Working a lot of overtime is not a demonstration of dedication. It's a consequence of bad planning, and often the result of manipulable developers being coerced into agreeing on unrealistic deadlines.
