# 01 A Pragmatic Philosophy (Tips 1–10)

The foundation. Before tools, before techniques — the mindset of a Pragmatic Programmer.

---

## Tip 1: Care About Your Craft

> Why spend your life developing software unless you care about doing it well?

You're not just churning out code. You're practicing a craft. Craftsmen care about the quality of their work — not because someone's watching, but because it matters to them.

---

## Tip 2: Think! About Your Work

> Turn off the autopilot. Every decision deserves conscious thought.

Don't just follow the pattern because "that's how we've always done it." Question. Evaluate. Choose deliberately.

---

## Tip 3: The Cat Ate My Source Code — Take Responsibility

> Provide options, don't make lame excuses.

When things go wrong, be honest. Instead of "It's not my fault — the library was broken," say "The library failed. Here are three things we can do: fix the library, find an alternative, or rewrite that component. Which do you prefer?"

| Lame Excuse | Professional Response |
|------------|---------------------|
| "The spec was wrong." | "I should have asked for clarification earlier." |
| "QA didn't test it." | "I should have written better tests." |
| "The deadline was impossible." | "I should have raised the issue when I saw it." |

> **Own your failures.** When you take responsibility, you gain the right to make things right.

---

## Tip 4: Don't Live with Broken Windows

> Don't leave "broken windows" (bad designs, wrong decisions, poor code) unrepaired. Fix each one as soon as it's discovered.

One broken window, left unrepaired, signals that nobody cares — and leads to more broken windows. A clean, well-maintained system signals that quality matters — and people treat it accordingly.

---

## Tip 5: Stone Soup and Boiled Frogs — Be a Catalyst for Change

### Stone Soup

Soldiers come to a village with nothing but a pot and a stone. They boil water with the stone. Curious villagers contribute a carrot, a potato, some meat. Soon, there's a feast. The lesson: **start with something small and simple, show it working, then ask for contributions.** People find it easier to join an ongoing success than to start from scratch.

### Boiled Frogs

A frog dropped into boiling water jumps out. A frog in cold water that's slowly heated boils to death without noticing. Don't be like the frog. **Keep watching the big picture.** Minor issues that accumulate unnoticed eventually destroy a project.

---

## Tip 6: Good-Enough Software

> Make quality a requirements issue.

Software doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to be good enough for its users, its maintainers, and its future. Involve users in the trade-off. "We can make it prettier, but that adds a week. Is that worth it?"

---

## Tip 7–8: Your Knowledge Portfolio

> Invest in your knowledge portfolio regularly. Knowledge assets expire.

| Investment Strategy | Software Equivalent |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Invest regularly | Read a technical book per quarter |
| Diversify | Learn multiple languages, paradigms, domains |
| Manage risk | Don't put all your knowledge in one technology |
| Buy low, sell high | Learn emerging tech before it's hot |
| Review and rebalance | Periodically assess your skills |

### Goals
- Learn at least one new language every year
- Read a technical book each quarter
- Read non-technical books too (communication, management, design)
- Take classes, go to user groups, participate in online communities
- Experiment with different environments (Linux, Windows, cloud)

---

## Tip 9: Critically Analyze What You Read and Hear

> Don't be a parrot. Question everything.

When vendors promise silver bullets, ask: "How? Show me." When consultants push a methodology, ask: "What problem does this solve?" When a blog post claims a framework is dead, ask: "According to whom, and in what context?"

---

## Tip 10: Communicate!

> It's both WHAT you say and the WAY you say it.

| Communication Principle | Why |
|------------------------|-----|
| Know your audience | Technical depth varies. Adjust. |
| Know what you want to say | Plan before speaking/writing |
| Choose your moment | Don't ambush. Schedule. |
| Choose a style | Formal report? Quick email? Whiteboard session? |
| Make it look good | Presentation matters. Proofread. |
| Involve your audience | Early drafts, feedback loops |
| Be a listener | Communication is two-way |
| Get back to people | "I'll get back to you" means GET BACK TO THEM |
