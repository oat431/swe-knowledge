# Teams and Projects

> *Source: The Clean Coder by Robert C. Martin, Chapter 13 (pp. 167–171)*

---

## Core Principle

> **Teams are harder to build than projects. Form persistent, gelled teams — then feed them projects. Never break up a gelled team just because a project ends. The team is the asset; projects are transient.**

---

## The Rules

### 1. There Is No Such Thing as Half a Person
Staffing people at 50% or 25% across multiple projects with different managers, analysts, and testers does not create a team. It creates a Waring blender — a fragmented mess where nobody learns to collaborate. Teams require full-time commitment to gel.

### 2. Let Teams Gel Before Expecting Magic
Teams need six months to a year to work out differences, learn each other's strengths and weaknesses, and build trust. Once gelled, a team anticipates each other, covers for each other, and demands the best from each other. Breaking up a gelled team because a project ends is ludicrous — keep it together and feed it the next project.

### 3. Build Teams Around Roles, Not Just Programmers
A properly gelled team of ~12 people includes programmers (7), testers (2), analysts (2), and a project manager (1). Analysts write acceptance tests focused on business value and the happy path; testers write acceptance tests focused on correctness, failure, and boundary cases. One member may also serve as a part-time coach — defending the team's process and disciplines under schedule pressure.

### 4. Allocate Projects to Teams, Not Teams to Projects
Banks and insurance companies form teams around projects — and it fails. Individuals never stay long enough to gel. Professional organizations invert this: they give projects to existing gelled teams. A gelled team can handle multiple projects simultaneously, distributing work by skill and ability.

### 5. Manage by Velocity, Not by Dedication
Velocity (points completed per week) is a statistical average. Management uses it to set targets across concurrent projects (e.g., 15/15/20 points split across three projects). The real advantage: a gelled team can pivot on a dime when the business says "Project B is in crisis — put 100% there for three weeks." Project-assembled teams cannot do this.

### 6. Let the Business Shift Priorities
Project owners may fear losing dedicated teams when resources can be reallocated. But the business should not have its hands tied by the artificial difficulty of forming and disbanding teams. If one project is higher priority, resources should move. It is the project owner's job to make the case for their project — not to rely on organizational inertia.

---

## Summary Checklist
- [ ] Never staff people at fractional capacity across unrelated projects — it prevents team formation
- [ ] Give new teams 6–12 months to gel; do not disband them when a project ends
- [ ] Ensure teams include programmers, testers, analysts, and a project manager
- [ ] Designate a process guardian (coach/master) to defend disciplines under pressure
- [ ] Allocate projects to persistent teams — never form teams around individual projects
- [ ] Track velocity as a statistical average; use it to set multi-project targets
- [ ] Structure teams to handle rapid priority shifts — gelled multi-project teams can pivot instantly
- [ ] As a project owner, defend your project on merit, not on the cost of reassignment
