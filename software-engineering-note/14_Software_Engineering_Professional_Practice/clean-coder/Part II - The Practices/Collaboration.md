# Collaboration

> *Source: The Clean Coder by Robert C. Martin, Chapter 12 (pp. 157–166)*

---

## Core Principle

> **Professional programming is not a solitary activity — it demands continuous collaboration with the business, your team, and other programmers. Knowledge silos and isolation are antithetical to professionalism.**

---

## The Rules

### 1. Collaborate with the Business
Professional programmers don't bury themselves in technology while the business crashes around them. They talk to users, sales, marketing, and managers to understand business goals. The first responsibility is meeting the employer's needs — understanding *why* you're writing the code and how the business benefits from it. Bugs aren't trophies to celebrate; they're problems that need fixing.

### 2. Break Down Code Ownership Walls
Owned code — where each programmer builds a wall around their module and refuses to let others touch it — is a recipe for disaster. It creates duplication, skewed interfaces, and political fiefdoms where compensation is tied to device importance rather than technical quality. Professional teams practice collective ownership: any team member can check out any module and make appropriate changes.

### 3. Pair Program
Professionals pair. Not just in emergencies (where everyone already admits two heads are better than one), but routinely. Pairing is the most efficient way to solve many problems, the best way to share knowledge across the team, and the most effective form of code review. It prevents knowledge silos and ensures every team member can play multiple positions.

### 4. Work Physically Together
Sitting in cubicles with headphones on, backs to each other, is not collaboration. Teams should sit around tables facing each other — able to overhear frustrations, read body language, and communicate serendipitously. The image of "rubbing cerebellums" (from a famously misguided recruiting billboard) is exactly what professionals avoid: the cerebellum controls fine motor skills, not intelligence, and rubbing cerebellums requires facing *away* from each other.

---

## The Cerebellum Principle

> **Collaboration isn't just a social nicety — it's a technical advantage. Pairing and close teamwork distribute knowledge, catch defects early, and prevent the fragility that comes from isolated expertise.**

When knowledge is siloed, the team is one bus factor away from disaster. When programmers pair, knowledge transfers naturally. Code gets reviewed *as it's written* — the most efficient form of review possible. And when the entire team collectively owns the code, architecture decisions benefit from multiple perspectives instead of one programmer's assumptions.

---

## Summary Checklist
- [ ] Understand the business goals behind the code you write
- [ ] Talk regularly to users, managers, and business stakeholders
- [ ] Reject code ownership walls — advocate collective team ownership
- [ ] Pair program routinely, not just in emergencies
- [ ] Share knowledge actively; avoid creating personal knowledge silos
- [ ] Work in a layout that enables serendipitous communication
- [ ] Recognize that your individual productivity matters less than team productivity
- [ ] Code review by collaborating in writing, not by formal inspection meetings
