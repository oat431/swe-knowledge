# 19 — Systems Engineering and Other Engineering Disciplines

> *Source: SEBoK v2, Part 6 — SE and Other Engineering Disciplines*

## Purpose

Systems Engineering is inherently interdisciplinary — it integrates inputs from and coordinates across multiple engineering disciplines. This Knowledge Area summarizes SE's relationship with key engineering fields, highlighting the interfaces, shared concerns, and what each discipline contributes to the system.

## SE and Environmental Engineering

Environmental engineering ensures systems comply with environmental regulations and minimize ecological impact. SE-SE interface includes: environmental requirements definition, life cycle assessment, sustainability analysis, hazardous material management, and environmental impact statements as part of system design and disposal.

## SE and Geospatial/Geodetic Engineering

Geospatial engineering provides the spatial data infrastructure for systems that depend on location — navigation, mapping, earth observation, and positioning systems. SE integration includes: geospatial data requirements, coordinate reference systems, accuracy specifications, and interface standards for geospatial data exchange.

## SE and Industrial Engineering

Industrial engineering optimizes complex processes and systems for efficiency, quality, and safety. Shared SE-IE concerns include: process design and optimization, facility layout and workflow, production system design, supply chain integration, quality management (Six Sigma, Lean), and human factors in manufacturing.

## SE and Aerospace Engineering

Aerospace engineering is one of the historical birthplaces of SE. The disciplines are deeply intertwined: SE provides the overarching framework for aerospace system development (requirements, architecture, integration, verification), while aerospace engineering contributes domain-specific expertise in aerodynamics, propulsion, structures, guidance/navigation/control, and space environments.

## SE and Electrical Engineering

Electrical engineering contributes expertise in: power systems, electronics, signal processing, communications, control systems, and electromagnetic compatibility. SE integrates electrical subsystems into the broader system architecture, manages electrical interfaces, and ensures electromagnetic compatibility across the system.

## SE and Mechanical Engineering

Mechanical engineering addresses the physical realization of systems: structures, mechanisms, thermal management, materials, and manufacturing. SE-ME interfaces include: physical architecture definition, mass/volume/power budgets, structural analysis integration, thermal management requirements, and design for manufacturability.

## SE and Civil Engineering

Civil engineering applies to large-scale infrastructure systems: transportation, water resources, buildings, and urban systems. SE brings a systematic approach to civil infrastructure — stakeholder needs analysis, requirements management, system-level optimization, life cycle costing, and integration of subsystems (structural, electrical, environmental, transportation).

## SE and Economics

Economics informs SE decision-making through: cost-benefit analysis, life cycle cost analysis, return on investment assessment, market analysis for product systems, affordability constraints, and economic evaluation of alternatives in trade studies. Understanding economic principles helps systems engineers make defensible decisions about system value.

## SE and Enterprise IT

Enterprise IT systems present unique SE challenges: rapid technology refresh, legacy system integration, diverse user populations, security and privacy requirements, scalability demands, and service-oriented architectures. SE contributes structured requirements, architecture, and integration approaches to enterprise IT, while learning from IT's agile, DevOps, and cloud-native practices.
