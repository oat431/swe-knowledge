# 00 — Introduction to the SEBoK

## Purpose

The **Guide to the Systems Engineering Body of Knowledge (SEBoK)** is a widely accepted, community-based, and regularly updated baseline of **systems engineering (SE)** knowledge. It is a curated, living authoritative guide — not a self-contained knowledge resource — that defines the boundaries, terminology, content, and structure of systems engineering. The SEBoK points readers to detailed sources for additional information.

The SEBoK serves **six broad purposes**:

| # | Purpose | Description |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | **Inform Practice** | Inform systems engineers about the boundaries, terminology, and structure of their discipline and point them to useful information needed to practice SE in any application domain. |
| 2 | **Inform Research** | Inform researchers about limitations and gaps in current SE knowledge to help guide their research agenda. |
| 3 | **Inform Interactors** | Inform performers in interacting disciplines (system implementation, project and enterprise management, other disciplines) and other stakeholders of the nature and value of SE. |
| 4 | **Inform Curriculum Developers** | Inform organizations defining the content that should be common in undergraduate and graduate programs in SE. |
| 5 | **Inform Certifiers** | Inform organizations certifying individuals as qualified to practice systems engineering. |
| 6 | **Inform SE Staffing** | Inform organizations and managers deciding which competencies practicing systems engineers should possess in various roles ranging from apprentice to expert. |

The SEBoK is **global in applicability**, authored and reviewed by experts from diverse locales and industries. It is designed for easy, continuous updating as new sources of knowledge emerge (currently updated on a semi-annual basis).

---

## Scope of the SEBoK

The SEBoK:

- Provides a **guide to the body of SE knowledge** with references to detailed sources; it is not a self-contained knowledge resource.
- Focuses on **engineered systems** (ES) contexts — socio-technical systems with a recognized SE life cycle — while treating social and natural systems as relevant environmental considerations (see [[02_Foundations_of_Systems_Engineering]]).
- Describes generic **SE life cycle and process knowledge** (see [[03_Systems_Engineering_and_Management]]).
- Recognizes that SE principles can be applied differently to different types of **products, services, enterprises, and systems of systems (SoS)** (see [[04_Applications_of_Systems_Engineering]]).
- Provides resources for **organization support** of SE activities (see [[05_Enabling_Systems_Engineering]]).
- Explores the **interaction between SE and other disciplines**, highlighting what systems engineers need to know about these disciplines (see [[06_Related_Disciplines]]).
- Is **domain-independent**, with implementation examples to provide domain-specific context (see [[07_Implementation_Examples]]).

Studies of system cost, schedule, and safety failures have shown that most failures come not from domain disciplines, but from **lack of adequate Systems Engineering** (NDIA 2003, 2006, 2016; GAO 2016; Leveson 2012). The SEBoK exists to strengthen mutual understanding across the many disciplines involved in developing and operating systems.

## Introduction to Systems Engineering — Chapter Overview

The **Introduction to Systems Engineering** knowledge area within Part 1 contains topic articles providing an overview of SE practice and discussing its economic value, historic evolution, and key relationships.

### Systems Engineering Overview

**Systems Engineering (SE)** is defined by INCOSE (2023) as:

> *"A transdisciplinary and integrative approach to enable the successful realization, use, and retirement of engineered systems, using systems principles and concepts, and scientific, technological, and management methods."*

Key elements include:

- **System**: An interacting combination of system elements (hardware, software, firmware, people, information, techniques, facilities, services, and other support elements) that accomplish defined objective(s).
- **Systems Engineer**: A person or role who supports the transdisciplinary approach, serving to elicit and translate customer needs into specifications that can be realized by the system development team.
- **Life Cycle Processes**: The systems engineer supports processes beginning early in conceptual design and continuing throughout the life cycle — manufacturing, deployment, use, and disposal. Activities include analyzing, specifying, designing, and verifying the system to balance functional, interface, performance, physical, quality, and cost characteristics.

The scope of SE does not include everything involved in engineering and management. Activities such as system construction, manufacturing, funding, and general management are part of the **SE environment** but not SE itself. SE is performed as part of a life cycle approach involving collaboration among systems engineers, systems developers, and project-external bodies (users, owners, external systems).

An **engineered system** is a technical or socio-technical system which is the subject of an SE life cycle. An **engineered system context** centers around an engineered system but also includes relationships with other engineered, social, or natural systems in defined environments.

### Fundamentals for Digital Engineering

**Digital Engineering (DE)** is a systemic intervention — a holistic approach to SE transformation involving **people, processes, data, and technology**. It is not just about technology, but a paradigm shift from traditional paper-based SE to an integrated digital approach leveraging authoritative sources of systems data and models as a continuum across disciplines.

The INCOSE Vision 2035 highlights key trends: digital transformation, sustainability, smart systems, complexity growth, and advancements in modeling and simulation. Future SE will move towards a fully **model-based SE (MBSE)** environment, with data science techniques infused into practice.

DE comprises four fundamental components (the DE Systemic Framework):

| Component | Focus |
|-----------|-------|
| **Why** — Strategic Intent | Aligning DE initiatives with broader organizational strategy; establishing goals and objectives for digital transformation. |
| **Who** — Digitally Enabled Workforce | Skills, roles, responsibilities, and capabilities needed; a shift in mindset toward collaborative agile approaches and continuous learning. |
| **What** — Data, Technology, and Knowledge | Integrated models, data as a critical asset, common mental models across stakeholders, and technologies (digital twins, AI, ML, IoT, modeling and simulation). |
| **How** — Processes and Systems | Model-based approaches leveraging MBSE; standardized digital representations; end-to-end digital representation of the enterprise. |

The key shift enabled by DE is a move to collaborative **data-driven problem-solving** and a "single source of truth" approach to decision-making.

### Economic Value of Systems Engineering

Investing in thorough SE results in highly cost-effective systems. Research shows:

- Shortfalls in SE lead to either cancellation of expensive systems or even more expensive systems in terms of total cost of ownership or loss of human life (documented in FAA Advanced Automation System, FBI Virtual Case File System, Hubble Space Telescope, and Therac-25 case studies).
- Analysis of 161 projects in the COCOMO II database shows a statistically significant increase in rework costs: **18% rework** for 10,000-SLOC projects vs. **91% rework** for 10,000,000-SLOC projects.
- Werner Gruhl's analysis of NASA projects shows a general correlation between SE investment levels and reduced cost overruns.
- The **Constructive Systems Engineering Cost Model (COSYSMO)** helps determine "how much SE is enough" by estimating person-months needed as a function of system size and 14 modifying factors.
- Medium-sized projects have relatively flat operating regions; very large projects pay extremely large penalties for neglecting thorough SE. The goal is to balance risks of under-investing in SE against those of over-investing ("analysis paralysis").

### A Brief History of Systems Engineering

- **1940s–1950s**: SE emerged during WWII to deal with new technologies. The first known use of the term was by Mervin J. Kelly (Bell Labs) in 1950. The first paper (Schlager, 1956) and textbook (Goode & Machol, 1957) appeared. Key themes: systems engineers were deeply immersed in application domains, used transdisciplinary analysis, and practiced informal systems thinking.
- **1960s–1970s**: Arthur Hall's *A Methodology for Systems Engineering* (1962) identified five traits of the ideal systems engineer. NASA's Apollo program demonstrated SE's value. MIL-STD-499 (1969) formalized system engineering management. MIL-STD-499A (1974) added the Systems Engineering Management Plan (SEMP).
- **1980s–1990s**: The National Council on Systems Engineering (NCOSE) was formed in 1989, later becoming the **International Council on Systems Engineering (INCOSE)** in 1992. The first INCOSE journal appeared in 1994; the *NASA Systems Engineering Handbook* in 1995; INCOSE's *Systems Engineering Handbook* v1 in 1997.
- **21st Century**: ISO published its first standard defining SE application and management in 2005. Related standards followed (ISO/IEC TR 24748-1:2010, 15288, 12207).

### Systems Engineering: Historic and Future Challenges

Challenges have evolved from organizing ancient cities, through the Industrial Revolution and WWII, to modern software-intensive and cyber-physical systems:

- The rise of **software** as a critical element led to Software Engineering as a closely related discipline.
- Awareness of **human factors** reoriented SE from traditional "hard" SE toward "soft" SE approaches: emergent requirements, concurrent definition of requirements and solutions, evolutionary development.
- Since 1990, rapidly increasing scale, dynamism, and vulnerabilities have created the need for lean, agile, iterative, and evolutionary SE approaches.
- **Systems of Systems (SoS)** and **Enterprise Systems Engineering (ESE)** approaches have emerged.
- Emerging technologies — AI, machine learning, IoT, digital thread, additive manufacturing — continue to challenge SE.
- **MBSE** has evolved to manage the breadth and complexity of systems information.
- The **INCOSE Vision 2025** sees future systems needing to respond to growing societal needs, harness technology innovations, and be engineered by an evolving, diverse workforce.

### Systems Engineering and Other Disciplines

SE is holistic and stakeholder value-oriented, in contrast to component-oriented, performance-oriented engineering disciplines.

**Engineering disciplines intertwined with SE:**
- **Software Engineering (SwE)**: Most system functionality is now implemented in software. Neither SwE nor SE is a subset of the other (see [[06_Related_Disciplines]]).
- **Human Factors Engineering**: From micro-ergonomics to macro-ergonomics; human-systems integration is critical.
- **Industrial Engineering**: Overlaps significantly with SE in the industrial domain but also includes manufacturing outside SE.

**Non-engineering disciplines:**
- **Technical Management (TM)**: A specialization of project management; SE and PM have significant common content but neither is a subset of the other (see [[06_Related_Disciplines]]).
- **Procurement and Acquisition**: Practitioners draw upon SE to determine scope and requirements, prepare RFPs, design source selection processes, and monitor progress.

Most good systems engineers are **"T-shaped" people** — having both a working knowledge of wider-system considerations and deep expertise in a relevant domain.

### Fundamentals for Future Systems Engineering

Future trends per the INCOSE Vision:

- **Broadening SE Application Domains**: SE will extend beyond traditional aerospace/defense into broader realms of engineered, natural, and social systems.
- **More Intelligent and Autonomous Systems**: Systems will become smarter, self-organized, sustainable, resource-efficient, robust, and safer.
- **Theoretical Foundations**: SE will be supported by a more encompassing foundation of theory and sophisticated model-based methods.

The evolution of SE moves from a total control model to a **shared responsibility model** — something more like "collective actualization." Systems will represent a combination and interplay of technology and social factors. Foundations must expand to include principles, theories, models, and patterns of the human, biological, social, and agent realm. Ethical considerations include how to allocate responsibility in a shared responsibility model.

## SEBoK Users and Uses — Overview

The SEBoK is intended for a wide variety of users. Six key **personas** represent different target audiences:

### Guidance for Systems Engineering Novices

For undergraduate students and those new to SE:

1. **Learn the basic terms**: system, engineered system, systems engineering, systems engineer.
2. **Get an overview**: read [[#Systems Engineering Overview]], [[#Economic Value of Systems Engineering]], [[#Systems Engineering and Other Disciplines]], and [[#Systems Engineering: Historic and Future Challenges]].
3. **Learn about systems**: Part 2 — What is a System?, Systems Science, Systems Thinking, Representing Systems with Models.
4. **Learn the systems approach**: the eight articles in Systems Approach Applied to Engineered Systems.
5. **Explore SE methods**: Part 3 — Life Cycle Models through Systems Engineering Standards.
6. **Explore applications**: Part 4 — Product, Service, Enterprise, and SoS SE.
7. **Read case studies**: Part 7 — scan case studies and vignettes in areas of interest.
8. **Later reading**: Part 6 (Related Disciplines) and Part 5 (Enabling SE).

### Guidance for Systems Engineers

For entry-level and experienced systems engineers, the SEBoK serves as:

- A **quick reference** for definitions, concepts, principles, and topic discussions.
- A source of **primary and additional references** for in-depth study.
- A way to avoid less reliable information on the Internet.
- A framework for building **shared vocabulary and perspectives** within teams.

The SEBoK can be navigated by keyword search, browsing the Outline/Table of Contents, or sequential reading. Part 7 case studies provide domain-specific context. Part 8 (Emerging Knowledge) describes trends like AI integration.

### Guidance for Engineers

For engineers from non-SE backgrounds (biomedical, civil, electrical, chemical, mechanical, software, etc.):

- Part 1 provides an overview of SE and the SEBoK.
- Part 2 highlights systems knowledge foundational to SE.
- Part 3 (Life Cycle Models, System Definition, System Realization, System Deployment and Use, Technical Management Processes) is highly important.
- Part 4 identifies SE activities for four kinds of engineered systems.
- Part 5 explains how engineers fit into the larger picture of enabling SE.
- Part 6 (SE and Other Disciplines) is key, especially SE and Software Engineering, SE and Quality Attributes.
- Part 7 illustrates SE practices in real settings.

Engineers should not skip management-sounding topics — they describe how SE orchestrates multiple disciplines, not administrative management.

### Guidance for Systems Engineering Customers

Customers of SE provide resources and receive SE products and services. The SEBoK helps customers understand:

- The relationship between SE, system development, and project management (Part 1).
- Life cycle models and customer participation in SE activity (Part 3).
- How SE varies by class of system (Part 4).
- How SE relates to project management, procurement, and specialty engineering (Part 6).
- Successes to emulate and failures to avoid (Part 7).

The quality of customer input is critical; systems engineers evaluate customer input to formulate an approach to defining and realizing the system.

### Guidance for Educators and Researchers

**Educators** use the SEBoK together with the **Graduate Reference Curriculum for Systems Engineering (GRCSE)** to:

- Develop complete SE curricula, individual courses, or assessment criteria.
- Assign readings for courses and supplemental references for student research.
- Use case studies and vignettes in the classroom.

Per GRCSE, 50% of a graduate program's knowledge should be based on the Core Body of Knowledge (CorBoK), with 50% from the broader SEBoK to encourage healthy variety.

**Researchers** use the SEBoK to learn about the state of the art, find summaries of the literature, and identify opportunities to advance SE through further research.

### Guidance for General Managers

For general managers who preside over system development projects, acquisitions, product lines, and organizations, the SEBoK helps them understand:

- The boundaries and synergies among SE, systems development, project management, and life cycle support (Part 1).
- How those boundaries evolve with lean, agile, and evolutionary methods.
- How to balance a mix of hardware, software, human factors, and domain specialists.
- Evolving system life cycle models and management of SE activity (Part 3).
- How SE varies by class of system (Part 4).
- SE governance and competence development (Part 5).
- How SE relates to software engineering, project management, procurement, and specialty engineering (Part 6).

The SEBoK complements the general management guidance available in sources such as the **PMBOK® Guide**.
