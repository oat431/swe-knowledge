# 08 — Techniques Catalog

> *Source: BABOK® Guide v3 by IIBA, Chapter 9 — Techniques*

## Purpose

This chapter catalogs the 50 techniques referenced throughout the BABOK® Guide. Each technique includes a brief description and identifies which business analysis tasks it supports. Use this as a quick reference when selecting the right approach for a given task.

---

## Acceptance and Evaluation Criteria
Criteria used to determine whether a solution or solution component meets the requirements. Acceptance criteria define the conditions that must be satisfied for a deliverable to be accepted by stakeholders. Evaluation criteria measure how well a solution meets broader goals.

## Backlog Management
An ordered list of work items (user stories, features, requirements) prioritized by business value. The backlog is continuously refined and reprioritized. Essential in adaptive (Agile) approaches.

## Balanced Scorecard
A strategic planning and management framework that translates an organization's vision into measurable objectives across four perspectives: Financial, Customer, Internal Business Process, and Learning & Growth.

## Benchmarking and Market Analysis
Comparing organizational practices, processes, or outcomes against industry best practices or competitors. Used to identify improvement opportunities and set performance targets.

## Brainstorming
A group creativity technique for generating a large number of ideas in a short time. Encourages free thinking without immediate judgment. Used for requirements elicitation, risk identification, and problem-solving.

## Business Capability Analysis
Modeling and analyzing an organization's capabilities (what it does) to identify gaps, redundancies, or opportunities. Capabilities are what an organization does, independent of how or who.

## Business Cases
A documented justification for undertaking a project or initiative. Includes: problem statement, options analysis, cost-benefit analysis, risk assessment, and recommendation. Used to secure funding and authorization.

## Business Model Canvas
A one-page visual framework describing how an organization creates, delivers, and captures value. Nine building blocks: Customer Segments, Value Propositions, Channels, Customer Relationships, Revenue Streams, Key Resources, Key Activities, Key Partnerships, Cost Structure.

## Business Rules Analysis
Identifying, documenting, and analyzing business rules that govern organizational decisions and behavior. Business rules define or constrain aspects of the business (policies, regulations, computations, derivations).

## Collaborative Games
Structured activities that encourage creative thinking and collaboration among stakeholders. Examples: Product Box, Speedboat, Fishbowl. Used in elicitation and problem-solving.

## Concept Modelling
Creating a shared understanding of core business concepts and their relationships. Uses a business-oriented vocabulary independent of technology. Foundation for requirements and data modelling.

## Data Dictionary
A centralized repository defining all data elements used in the organization or solution. Includes: name, description, type, length, valid values, source, owner. Ensures consistent terminology.

## Data Flow Diagrams (DFD)
A graphical representation of how data moves through a system. Shows external entities, processes, data stores, and data flows. Focuses on data movement, not control flow.

## Data Mining
Discovering patterns, correlations, and insights from large datasets using statistical and machine learning techniques. Used for decision support, trend analysis, and predictive analytics.

## Data Modelling
Defining the structure, relationships, and constraints of data used by an organization or system. Includes: Entity-Relationship Diagrams (ERD), class diagrams, and logical/physical data models.

## Decision Analysis
A structured approach to evaluating and comparing alternatives. Involves: defining the decision, identifying criteria, weighting criteria, scoring options, and analyzing results. Supports informed decision-making.

## Decision Modelling
Representing business decisions and their dependencies using decision tables, decision trees, or DMN (Decision Model and Notation). Clarifies complex decision logic.

## Document Analysis
Reviewing existing documentation to identify relevant information about the current state, requirements, or business processes. Efficient way to leverage existing knowledge.

## Estimation
Forecasting the effort, cost, or duration required for BA activities or solution components. Techniques: analogous, parametric, bottom-up, three-point. Used in planning.

## Financial Analysis
Evaluating the financial viability of initiatives or solutions. Techniques: Net Present Value (NPV), Return on Investment (ROI), Internal Rate of Return (IRR), payback period, cost-benefit analysis.

## Focus Groups
Facilitated discussions with a small group of stakeholders to explore ideas, attitudes, and perceptions about a product, service, or opportunity. Used in elicitation.

## Functional Decomposition
Breaking down a complex system, process, or problem into smaller, more manageable components. Top-down approach that reveals structure and relationships.

## Glossary
A shared repository of business and technical terms with agreed-upon definitions. Prevents misunderstandings and ensures consistent communication across stakeholders.

## Interface Analysis
Identifying and specifying interfaces between systems, components, or organizations. Documents inputs, outputs, protocols, frequency, and data formats.

## Interviews
One-on-one or small group conversations with stakeholders to elicit information about needs, expectations, and concerns. Can be structured, semi-structured, or unstructured.

## Item Tracking
Recording and managing issues, defects, risks, action items, and decisions throughout the BA effort. Ensures nothing is lost and accountability is clear.

## Lessons Learned
Capturing insights and experiences from completed work to improve future performance. Includes: what worked, what didn't, and recommendations for next time.

## Metrics and Key Performance Indicators (KPIs)
Defining quantifiable measures to track performance against objectives. Metrics are measurements; KPIs are the specific metrics that matter most for strategic goals.

## Mind Mapping
A visual technique for organizing information around a central concept. Hierarchical, radiating structure. Used for brainstorming, requirements organization, and problem analysis.

## Non-Functional Requirements Analysis
Identifying, documenting, and analyzing quality-of-service requirements: performance, security, reliability, usability, maintainability, scalability, and compliance.

## Observation
Watching stakeholders perform their work to understand processes, pain points, and opportunities. Can be passive (unobtrusive) or active (participatory, asking questions).

## Organizational Modelling
Representing the structure, roles, relationships, and culture of an organization. Includes: org charts, RACI matrices, stakeholder maps, influence diagrams.

## Prioritization
Ranking requirements, features, or initiatives based on agreed-upon criteria (value, urgency, risk, dependency). Techniques: MoSCoW, weighted ranking, Kano analysis, timeboxing.

## Process Analysis
Examining current processes to identify inefficiencies, bottlenecks, and improvement opportunities. Typically uses process models and root cause analysis.

## Process Modelling
Creating visual representations of business processes using notations like BPMN, flowcharts, or activity diagrams. Shows sequence, decision points, roles, and handoffs.

## Prototyping
Creating simplified versions of a solution to explore requirements, validate design decisions, and get early feedback. Types: low-fidelity (sketches, wireframes), high-fidelity (interactive).

## Reviews
Structured examination of BA deliverables (requirements, models, documents) by stakeholders for correctness, completeness, and quality. Types: informal walkthroughs to formal inspections.

## Risk Analysis and Management
Identifying, analyzing, and planning responses to risks that may affect the BA effort or solution success. Includes: risk identification, probability/impact assessment, mitigation planning.

## Roles and Permissions Matrix
Defining which roles have which permissions (create, read, update, delete, execute) on which system components. Basis for access control and security requirements.

## Root Cause Analysis
A systematic approach to identifying the underlying cause of a problem, not just the symptoms. Techniques: Five Whys, fishbone (Ishikawa) diagrams, fault tree analysis.

## Scope Modelling
Defining the boundaries of the solution or change initiative. Techniques: context diagrams, feature models, use case diagrams. Clarifies what's in and out of scope.

## Sequence Diagrams
UML diagrams showing how objects or actors interact in a particular sequence. Shows the order of messages exchanged over time. Used to specify interaction requirements.

## Stakeholder List, Map, or Personas
Identifying and categorizing stakeholders based on their interest, influence, and impact. Personas are archetypal user descriptions that guide requirements and design.

## State Modelling
Representing the possible states of an entity and the transitions between states. UML state machine diagrams show triggers, guards, and actions. Essential for complex lifecycle requirements.

## Survey or Questionnaire
Collecting information from a large group of stakeholders using structured questions. Efficient for quantitative data; less effective for deep exploration.

## SWOT Analysis
Evaluating Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, and Threats. Strengths/Weaknesses are internal; Opportunities/Threats are external. Used in strategy analysis and planning.

## Use Cases and Scenarios
Describing how actors interact with a system to achieve a goal. Use case includes: primary flow, alternative flows, pre/post conditions. Scenarios are specific instances.

## User Stories
Concise descriptions of functionality from an end-user perspective. Format: "As a [role], I want [capability] so that [benefit]." Includes acceptance criteria. Core to Agile approaches.

## Vendor Assessment
Evaluating potential vendors against defined criteria (capability, cost, reliability, support) to support make-or-buy and procurement decisions.

## Workshops
Facilitated group sessions focused on achieving a specific objective: requirements discovery, process design, problem-solving. Structured agenda, defined outputs, facilitated discussions.
