# Underlying Competencies

> *Source: BABOK® Guide v3 by IIBA, Chapter 9 — Underlying Competencies*

## Purpose

Underlying competencies are the foundational skills, knowledge, and behaviours that enable business analysts to perform their work effectively. Unlike the task-based knowledge areas, these competencies describe *how* business analysts think, behave, and interact — they underpin every business analysis activity regardless of domain, methodology, or organizational context. The six competency areas are: Analytical Thinking and Problem Solving, Behavioural Characteristics, Business Knowledge, Communication Skills, Interaction Skills, and Tools and Technology.

---

## Analytical Thinking and Problem Solving

> **Purpose:** Equip business analysts to analyze problems and opportunities effectively, identify which changes deliver the most value, and work with stakeholders to understand the impact of those changes.

Analytical thinking involves rapidly assimilating various types of information (diagrams, stakeholder concerns, customer feedback, schematics, spreadsheets) and identifying what is relevant. It enables business analysts to choose effective, adaptable methods for each situation and present information in forms best suited to their audience — for example, using graphics instead of text where concepts are more easily understood visually.

### Key Elements

#### Creative Thinking (9.1.1)
- **Purpose:** Generate new ideas, approaches, and alternatives for problem solving and opportunities.
- **Definition:** Involves generating new ideas and concepts, finding new associations between existing ideas, and questioning conventional approaches. Business analysts promote creative thinking by identifying alternatives, asking questions, and challenging assumptions. Techniques include combining, changing, and reapplying existing concepts.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Generating and productively considering new ideas
  - Exploring new concepts and changes to existing concepts
  - Generating creativity for self and others
  - Applying new ideas to resolve existing problems

#### Decision Making (9.1.2)
- **Purpose:** Understand the criteria involved in making a decision and assist others to make better decisions.
- **Definition:** When faced with selecting from alternatives, decision making involves gathering relevant information, analyzing it, making comparisons and trade-offs, and identifying the most desirable option. Business analysts document decisions and their rationale for future reference.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Appropriate stakeholders are represented in the decision-making process
  - Stakeholders understand the decision-making process and rationale
  - Pros and cons of all options are clearly communicated
  - The decision reduces or eliminates uncertainty
  - The decision addresses the need and is in the best interest of all stakeholders
  - A decision is actually made

#### Learning (9.1.3)
- **Purpose:** Quickly absorb new and different types of information and adapt existing knowledge to work effectively in rapidly changing environments.
- **Definition:** Learning is the process of gaining knowledge or skills, progressing through stages: acquisition of raw facts → comprehension → application → analysis, synthesis, and evaluation. Learning techniques include **visual** (pictures, diagrams, models, videos), **auditory** (verbal and written language), and **kinesthetic** (learning by doing). Most people retain information longer when multiple techniques are combined.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Understanding that learning is a process for all stakeholders
  - Demonstrating understanding of concepts presented
  - Applying concepts to new areas or relationships
  - Rapidly absorbing new facts, ideas, concepts, and opinions
  - Effectively presenting new information to others

#### Problem Solving (9.1.4)
- **Purpose:** Define and solve problems to ensure the real, underlying root cause is understood by all stakeholders and that solution options address that root cause.
- **Definition:** Involves ensuring the nature of the problem and underlying issues are clearly understood. Stakeholder viewpoints are articulated and conflicts between goals are addressed. Assumptions are identified and validated. Objectives that will be met once the problem is solved are specified. Alternatives are measured against objectives to determine the best solution and identify value/trade-offs.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Confidence of participants in the problem-solving process
  - Selected solutions meet defined objectives and solve the root cause
  - New solution options can be evaluated effectively using the problem-solving framework
  - Avoids decisions based on unvalidated assumptions or preconceived notions

#### Systems Thinking (9.1.5)
- **Purpose:** Understand how people, processes, and technology within an organization interact, providing a holistic point of view.
- **Definition:** Systems theory holds that a system as a whole has properties, behaviours, and characteristics that emerge from the interaction of its components — not predictable from understanding components alone. The term *system* includes people, interactions between them, external forces, and all other relevant elements.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Communicating how a change to a component affects the system as a whole
  - Communicating how a change to a system affects its environment
  - Communicating how systems adapt to internal and/or external pressures and changes

#### Conceptual Thinking (9.1.6)
- **Purpose:** Find ways to understand how large amounts of detailed and disparate information fits into a larger picture, identifying what details are important and connecting seemingly abstract information.
- **Definition:** Understanding the linkage between contexts, solutions, needs, changes, stakeholders, and value abstractly and in the big picture. Involves connecting information and patterns not obviously related, using past experiences, knowledge, creativity, intuition, and abstract thinking to generate alternatives not easily defined.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Connecting disparate information and acting to better understand the relationship
  - Confirming the confidence and understanding of the concept with stakeholders
  - Formulating abstract concepts using a combination of information and uncertainty
  - Drawing on past experiences to understand the situation

#### Visual Thinking (9.1.7)
- **Purpose:** Communicate complex concepts and models into understandable visual representations to engage stakeholders and help them understand the concepts being presented.
- **Definition:** Creating graphical representations of concepts or systems so stakeholders can easily understand them and provide input. Visual thinking requires making abstractions and finding suitable graphic devices to represent them. Visuals allow stakeholders to learn more quickly, process information, and connect points from each of their contexts.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Complex information is communicated in a visual model understandable by stakeholders
  - Visuals allow for comparisons, pattern finding, and idea mapping
  - Productivity increases due to increased learning and quick memory from effective visuals
  - Stakeholders are engaged at a deeper level than with text alone
  - Stakeholders understand critical information that may have been missed if presented as text only

## Behavioural Characteristics

> **Purpose:** The skills and behaviours that allow a business analyst to gain the trust and respect of stakeholders by consistently acting ethically, completing tasks on time, efficiently delivering quality results, and demonstrating adaptability.

These characteristics are not unique to business analysis but have been found to increase personal effectiveness in its practice. They exist at the core of every business analyst's skill set.

### Key Elements

#### Ethics (9.2.1)
- **Purpose:** Earn the respect of stakeholders and recognize when a proposed solution may present ethical difficulties, helping reduce exposure to risk.
- **Definition:** Requires understanding and focus on fairness, consideration, and moral behaviour. Includes considering the impact of proposed solutions on all stakeholder groups and ensuring fair treatment. Fair treatment does not require a beneficial outcome for every group, but requires that affected stakeholders understand the reasons for decisions.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Prompt identification and resolution of ethical dilemmas
  - Stakeholder feedback confirming decisions and actions are transparent and fair
  - Decisions made with consideration of all stakeholders' interests
  - Reasoning for decisions is clearly articulated and understood
  - Full and prompt disclosure of potential conflicts of interest
  - Honesty regarding one's abilities, work performance, and accepting responsibility for failures or errors

#### Personal Accountability (9.2.2)
- **Purpose:** Ensure business analysis tasks are completed on time and to expectations, establishing credibility by ensuring efforts meet business needs.
- **Description:** Includes effectively planning business analysis work to achieve targets and goals, ensuring value aligns with business needs. Involves chasing down all leads and loose ends to fully satisfy stakeholder needs. Business analysts take responsibility for identifying and escalating risks and issues, and ensure decision makers have appropriate information to assess impact.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Work effort is planned and easily articulated to others
  - Work is completed as planned or re-planned with sufficient reasoning and lead time
  - Status of both planned and unplanned work is known
  - Stakeholders feel that work is organized
  - Risks and issues are identified and appropriately acted on
  - Completely traceable requirements are delivered on time, and stakeholder needs are met

#### Trustworthiness (9.2.3)
- **Purpose:** Elicit business analysis information around sensitive issues and give stakeholders confidence that recommendations will be evaluated properly and fairly.
- **Description:** Trustworthiness may offset the natural fear of change experienced by many stakeholders. Contributing factors include: completing tasks on time and within budget (dependability), presenting a confident demeanour, acting honestly and transparently, addressing conflict immediately, and maintaining a consistent, predictable schedule.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Stakeholders involve the business analyst in discussions and decision making
  - Stakeholders bring issues and concerns to the business analyst
  - Stakeholders are willing to discuss difficult or controversial topics
  - Stakeholders do not blame the business analyst when problems occur
  - Stakeholders respect the business analyst's ideas and referrals
  - Stakeholders respond to the business analyst's referrals with positive feedback

#### Organization and Time Management (9.2.4)
- **Purpose:** Perform tasks effectively and use work time efficiently.
- **Description:** Involves prioritizing tasks, performing them efficiently, and managing time effectively. Business analysts accumulate significant information that must be organized and stored for reuse, and must differentiate important from less important information. Techniques include establishing short- and long-term goals, action plans, checklists, time limits on non-critical tasks, and managing interruptions.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Ability to produce deliverables in a timely manner
  - Stakeholders feel the business analyst focuses on the correct tasks at the right time
  - Schedule of work and deadlines is managed and communicated
  - Stakeholders feel their time in meetings and communications is well spent
  - Complete preparation for meetings, interviews, and requirements workshops
  - Relevant business analysis information is captured, organized, and documented
  - Adherence to project schedule and meeting deadlines
  - Maintains up-to-date information on status of each work item

#### Adaptability (9.2.5)
- **Purpose:** Adjust behavioural style and method of approach to increase effectiveness when interacting with different stakeholders, organizations, and situations in rapidly changing environments.
- **Definition:** The ability to change techniques, style, methods, and approach. Business analysts modify how they interact — some stakeholders are visual, others verbal. When organizational goals change or unanticipated problems occur, business analysts adapt by altering plans and identifying options to deliver maximum value.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Demonstrating the courage to act differently from others
  - Adapting to changing conditions and environments
  - Valuing and considering other points of view and approaches
  - Demonstrating a positive attitude in the face of ambiguity and change
  - Willingness to learn new methods, procedures, or techniques
  - Changing behaviour to perform effectively under changing or unclear conditions
  - Evaluating what worked, what did not, and what could be done differently next time

## Business Knowledge

> **Purpose:** Enable the business analyst to perform effectively within their business, industry, organization, solution, and methodology. Business knowledge provides understanding of the overarching concepts that govern structure, benefits, and value as they relate to a change or a need.

### Key Elements

#### Business Acumen (9.3.1)
- **Purpose:** Understand fundamental business principles and best practices to ensure they are considered as solutions are reviewed.
- **Description:** The ability to understand business needs using experience and knowledge from other situations. Organizations share similar practices (legal/regulatory, finance, logistics, sales, marketing, supply chain, HR, technology). Business acumen is applying knowledge of these commonalities across differing situations. Factors causing differences include industry, location, organization size, culture, and maturity.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Recognizing potential limitations and opportunities
  - Recognizing when changes to a situation may require a change in initiative direction
  - Understanding risks involved and ability to make decisions on managing risks
  - Recognizing opportunities to decrease expenses and increase profits
  - Understanding options available to address emerging changes

#### Industry Knowledge (9.3.2)
- **Purpose:** Understand current practices and activities within an industry and similar processes across industries.
- **Description:** Understanding of: current trends, market forces, market drivers, key processes, services, products, definitions, customer segments, suppliers, practices, regulations, and other impacting factors. Also includes understanding how a company is positioned within an industry and its impacts and dependencies. Key guiding questions include: Who are the top leaders? Which organizations promote/regulate the industry? Who are the target customers? What are the seasonal cycles?
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Awareness of activities within the enterprise and the broader industry
  - Knowledge of major competitors and partners
  - Ability to identify key trends shaping the industry
  - Familiarity with largest customer segments
  - Knowledge of common products and product types
  - Knowledge of sources of industry information (trade organizations, journals)
  - Understanding of industry-specific terms, standards, processes, and methodologies
  - Understanding of the industry regulatory environment

#### Organization Knowledge (9.3.3)
- **Purpose:** Understand the management structure and business architecture of the enterprise.
- **Definition:** Includes understanding how the enterprise generates profits, accomplishes goals, its organizational structure, relationships between business units, and persons in key stakeholder positions. Also includes understanding formal and informal communication channels and awareness of internal politics that influence decision making.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Ability to act according to informal and formal communications and authority channels
  - Understanding of terminology or jargon used in the organization
  - Understanding of the products or services offered by the organization
  - Ability to identify subject matter experts (SMEs) in the organization
  - Ability to navigate organizational relationships and politics

#### Solution Knowledge (9.3.4)
- **Purpose:** Leverage understanding of existing departments, environments, or technology to efficiently identify the most effective means of implementing a change.
- **Definition:** When improving an existing solution, business analysts apply knowledge from previous work on that solution. Familiarity with commercially available solutions or suppliers assists with identifying alternatives. Prior experiences can expedite discovery of potential changes through elicitation or in-depth analysis.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Reduced time or cost to implement a required change
  - Shortened time on requirements analysis and/or solution design
  - Understanding when a larger change is, or is not, justified based on business benefit
  - Understanding how additional capabilities present but not currently used can be deployed to provide value

#### Methodology Knowledge (9.3.5)
- **Purpose:** Understand the methodologies used by the organization to gain information about context, dependencies, opportunities, and constraints when developing a business analysis approach.
- **Description:** Methodologies determine the timing (big steps vs. small increments), approach, roles, accepted risk level, and other aspects of how change is approached and managed. Organizations adopt or create methodologies to fit varying levels of culture, maturity, adaptability, risk, uncertainty, and governance. Knowledge of a variety of methodologies allows quick adaptation to new environments.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Ability to adapt to changes in methodologies
  - Willingness to use or learn a new methodology
  - Successful integration of business analysis tasks and techniques to support the current methodology
  - Familiarity with the terms, tools, and techniques prescribed by a methodology
  - Ability to play multiple roles within activities prescribed by a methodology

## Communication Skills

> **Purpose:** Enable the effective conveyance of information from sender to receiver in a method that delivers the meaning the sender intended. Effective communication benefits all stakeholders.

Communication may be verbal, non-verbal, physical, and written. Effective communication requires both sender and receiver to share the same understanding. A shared glossary and clear goals help avoid misunderstandings. Planning effective communication includes considering what the receiver knows, structuring information logically, determining the best presentation method, and understanding recipient expectations.

### Key Elements

#### Verbal Communication (9.4.1)
- **Purpose:** Convey ideas, concepts, facts, and opinions to a variety of stakeholders.
- **Description:** Uses spoken words to convey information. Deals specifically with word choice and tone of voice. When the receiver is visible, non-verbal cues impact meaning. Monitoring the receiver's non-verbal communication allows the sender to adapt the message. Effective verbal skills include making one's meaning understood and pairing with active listening.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Restating concepts to ensure all stakeholders clearly understand the same information
  - Assisting conversations to reach productive conclusions
  - Delivering effective presentations by designing and positioning content and objectives appropriately
  - Communicating an issue's important points in a calm and rational manner, presenting solution options

#### Non-Verbal Communication (9.4.2)
- **Purpose:** Effectively send and receive messages through body movement, posture, facial expressions, gestures, and eye contact.
- **Definition:** Non-verbal communication is believed to convey much more meaning than words alone. Moods, attitudes, and feelings impact body movement and facial expressions. Effective use can present a trustworthy, confident, and capable demeanour. When a stakeholder's non-verbal communication disagrees with their verbal message, the business analyst should explore further to uncover the source of disagreement.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Being aware of body language in others, but not assuming complete understanding through non-verbal communication alone
  - Intentional awareness of personal non-verbal communication
  - Improving trust and communication as a result of non-verbal communication
  - Effectively addressing situations when non-verbal and verbal messages disagree

#### Written Communication (9.4.3)
- **Purpose:** Convey ideas, concepts, facts, and opinions to a variety of stakeholders using text, symbols, models, and sketches.
- **Definition:** The practice of using text, symbols, models (formal or informal), and sketches to convey and share information. Requires a broad vocabulary, strong grasp of grammar and style, and understanding of terms understood by the audience. Written communication has the added challenge of presenting information remotely from the time and place it was created.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Adjusting the style of writing for the needs of the audience
  - Proper use of grammar and style
  - Choosing words the audience will understand the intended meaning of
  - Ability of the reader to paraphrase and describe the content of the written communication

#### Listening (9.4.4)
- **Purpose:** Accurately understand information that is communicated verbally.
- **Definition:** Listening is not just hearing words but understanding their meaning in context. Active listening involves both listening and interpreting what the other person is trying to communicate beyond the words used. It includes summarizing and repeating what was stated in different terms to ensure both listener and speaker have the same understanding.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Giving the speaker undivided attention
  - Acknowledging the speaker with verbal or non-verbal encouragement
  - Providing feedback to ensure understanding
  - Using active listening skills by deferring judgment and responding appropriately

## Interaction Skills

> **Purpose:** Enable the business analyst to relate, cooperate, and communicate with different kinds of people including executives, sponsors, colleagues, team members, developers, vendors, end users, customers, and subject matter experts (SMEs).

Business analysts are uniquely positioned to facilitate stakeholder communication, provide leadership, encourage comprehension of solution value, and promote stakeholder support of proposed changes.

### Key Elements

#### Facilitation (9.5.1)
- **Purpose:** Help stakeholders make a decision, solve a problem, exchange ideas and information, or reach agreement regarding the priority and nature of requirements.
- **Definition:** The skill of moderating discussions within a group to enable all participants to effectively articulate their views and ensure participants recognize and appreciate differing points of view. The facilitator is a third party — not a decision maker or the owner of the topic.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Making it clear that the facilitator is a third party, not a decision maker or topic owner
  - Encouraging participation from all attendees
  - Remaining neutral while being impartial and intervening when required
  - Establishing ground rules (openness to suggestions, building on what is there, not dismissing ideas, allowing others to speak)
  - Ensuring participants correctly understand each other's positions
  - Using meeting management skills and tools to keep discussions focused and organized
  - Preventing discussions from being sidetracked onto irrelevant topics
  - Understanding and considering all parties' interests, motivations, and objectives

#### Leadership and Influencing (9.5.2)
- **Purpose:** Guide stakeholders during investigation of business analysis information and solution options, building consensus and encouraging stakeholder support and collaboration during change.
- **Definition:** Motivating people to act in ways that enable them to work together to achieve shared goals and objectives. Understanding individual motives, needs, and capabilities of each stakeholder assists in meeting shared objectives. The business analyst's responsibility for defining, analyzing, and communicating business analysis information provides leadership opportunities whether or not people formally report to them.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Reduced resistance to necessary changes
  - Articulation of a clear and inspiring vision of a desired future state
  - Success in inspiring others to turn vision into action
  - Influence on stakeholders to understand mutual interests
  - Effective use of collaboration techniques to influence others
  - Influence on stakeholders to consider broader objectives over personal motivations
  - Re-framing issues so alternate perspectives can be understood and accommodated

#### Teamwork (9.5.3)
- **Purpose:** Work productively with team members, stakeholders, and other vested partners so that solutions can be effectively developed and implemented.
- **Definition:** Business analysts often work as part of a team. Relationships are critical to project success. It is important to understand how a team is formed, how it functions, and team dynamics across project stages. Building and maintaining trust contributes to team integrity. Team conflict is common; if handled well, resolution can benefit the team by strengthening the foundation of analysis and solution.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Fostering a collaborative working environment
  - Effectively resolving conflict
  - Developing trust among team members
  - Support among the team for shared high standards of achievement
  - Promoting a shared sense of ownership of the team goals

#### Negotiation and Conflict Resolution (9.5.4)
- **Purpose:** Mediate negotiations between stakeholders to reach common understanding or agreement, resolving conflicts while maintaining and strengthening working relationships.
- **Definition:** Mediating discussions to help participants recognize differing views, resolve differences, and reach conclusions with agreement of all participants. Successful negotiation includes identifying the underlying interests of parties, distinguishing interests from stated positions, and helping parties identify solutions that satisfy those underlying interests — while ensuring the outcome aligns with the overall solution and business needs.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - A planned approach accounting for tone of voice, conveyed attitude, methods used, and concern for the other side's feelings and needs
  - Recognizing that parties' needs are not always in opposition — often possible to satisfy both without either losing
  - An objective approach separating the problem from the person so real issues are debated without damaging working relationships
  - Recognizing that effective negotiation is not always achieved in a single meeting — sometimes several meetings are required

#### Teaching (9.5.5)
- **Purpose:** Effectively communicate business analysis information, concepts, ideas, and issues, ensuring they are understood and retained by stakeholders.
- **Definition:** Teaching is leading others to gain knowledge. Business analysts confirm information has been understood by stakeholders and lead them to discover clarity in ambiguity. This requires selecting the most appropriate visual, verbal, written, and kinesthetic teaching approaches. Business analysts frequently elicit and learn new information, then teach it to stakeholders in a meaningful way.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Utilizing different methods to communicate information to be learned by stakeholders
  - Discovering new information through high levels of stakeholder engagement
  - Validating that audiences have a clear understanding of the key messages intended to be learned
  - Verifying that stakeholders can demonstrate the new knowledge, facts, concepts, and ideas

## Tools and Technology

> **Purpose:** Leverage software applications to support communication and collaboration, create and maintain requirements artifacts, model concepts, track issues, and increase overall productivity.

Business analysts use word processing for documentation, prototyping and simulation tools for developing requirements, specialized tools for modelling and diagramming, requirements management technologies for workflow/approvals/baselining/change control, communication tools for stakeholder interaction, and presentation software to showcase ideas.

### Key Elements

#### Office Productivity Tools and Technology (9.6.1)
- **Purpose:** Document and track information and artifacts.
- **Definition:** Tools to organize, dissect, manipulate, understand, and communicate information clearly. Includes:
  - **Word processing and presentation programs:** Present information as letters, reports, slide decks, animations. Commonly used for requirements documents with templates, limited change tracking, and collaborative features (via cloud solutions).
  - **Presentation software:** Create training materials and stimulate discussion. Can be used in a limited way to capture requirements or create basic prototypes.
  - **Spreadsheets:** Allow mathematical and logical manipulation. Used to maintain lists (requirements, features, actions, issues, defects), capture and manipulate numeric data, support decision analysis, and summarize complex scenarios.
  - **Communication tools (email and instant messaging):** Communicate with remote stakeholders who cannot respond immediately or need long-term records. Easy to use but generally not effective for long-term storage.
  - **Collaboration and knowledge management tools:** Capture distributed knowledge and make it widely available (document repositories, wikis, discussion forums, cloud services). Enable multiple users to work simultaneously with comments and discussion.
  - **Hardware:** Printers, digital projectors, photocopiers, and scanners to replicate and distribute information.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Increased efficiencies and streamlined processes by exploring tool features and functions
  - Awareness of available tools, their operation, and abilities
  - Ability to determine the tool that will best meet stakeholder needs
  - Ability to clearly communicate the major features of available tools

#### Business Analysis Tools and Technology (9.6.2)
- **Purpose:** Model, document, and manage outputs of business analysis activities and deliverables to stakeholders.
- **Definition:** Specialized tools for modelling, diagramming, documenting, analyzing/mapping requirements, identifying relationships between requirements, tracking/storing requirements artifacts, and communicating with stakeholders. Categories include:
  - **Modelling tools:** Create models and visuals to align stakeholders and outline relationships; trace visuals to business rules and requirements; create executable models or generate application code. Some support compliance validation with notation (e.g., BPMN) and executable models (BPMS, BRMS).
  - **Requirements management technologies:** Support requirements workflow (baselining, approvals, sign-off, change control, implementation status), traceability (backwards, forwards, relationships, impact analysis), configuration management, and quality verification.
  - **Issue tracking tools:** Track requirements risks, requirements conflicts and issues, and defects.
  - **Prototyping and simulation tools:** Prototype or simulate the solution or pieces of the solution.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Ability to apply understanding of one tool to other similar tools
  - Identifying major tools currently available and describing their strengths, weaknesses, and uses
  - Understanding and ability to use the major features of the tool
  - Ability to select tools that support organizational processes
  - Ability to use tools to complete requirements-related activities more rapidly than otherwise possible
  - Ability to track changes to requirements and their impact on solution implementation, stakeholders, and value

#### Communication Tools and Technology (9.6.3)
- **Purpose:** Perform business analysis activities, manage teams, and collaborate with stakeholders.
- **Definition:** Tools for conversational and collaborative interactions, enabling work with virtual and co-located teams. Business analysts select the appropriate tool balancing cost, risk, and value. Categories include:
  - **Conversation interaction tools:** Voice communications, instant messaging, online chat, email, blogging, microblogging.
  - **Collaboration tools:** Video conferencing, electronic whiteboarding, wikis, electronic calendars, online brainstorming tools, electronic decision making, electronic voting, document sharing, idea sharing.
- **Effectiveness Measures:**
  - Selection of appropriate and effective tools for the audience and purpose
  - Effectively choosing when to use communication technology and when not to
  - Ability to identify tools to meet communication needs
  - Understanding and ability to use features of the tool
