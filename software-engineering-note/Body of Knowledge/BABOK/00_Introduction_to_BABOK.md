# Introduction to BABOK® Guide v3

> *Source: BABOK® Guide v3 by IIBA (International Institute of Business Analysis), Chapter 1 — Introduction, Chapter 2 — Business Analysis Key Concepts*

## Purpose

The BABOK® Guide (Business Analysis Body of Knowledge) is the globally recognized standard for the practice of business analysis, maintained by the International Institute of Business Analysis (IIBA). It describes business analysis tasks organized into knowledge areas, defines the key concepts and competencies required for effective business analysis, and provides a common terminology for the profession.

The Guide serves to:

- Define the profession and scope of business analysis
- Provide a common framework and language for business analysts across industries, methodologies, and organizational levels
- Describe the generally accepted tasks, techniques, and competencies that constitute the practice
- Support certification, role definition, and professional development

## What is Business Analysis?

**Business analysis** is the practice of enabling change in an enterprise by defining needs and recommending solutions that deliver value to stakeholders. It enables an enterprise to articulate needs and the rationale for change, and to design and describe solutions that can deliver value.

Business analysis is performed on strategic, tactical, and operational initiatives. It can be conducted within the boundaries of a project or throughout enterprise evolution and continuous improvement. It is used to:

- Understand the **current state**
- Define the **future state**
- Determine the **activities required** to move from the current to the future state

Business analysis may be practiced from diverse **perspectives**, including agile, business intelligence, information technology, business architecture, and business process management. A perspective is a lens through which the practitioner views their work based on the current context. One or multiple perspectives may apply to any initiative.

### Who is a Business Analyst?

A **business analyst** is any person who performs business analysis tasks described in the BABOK® Guide, regardless of job title or organizational role. Business analysts are responsible for discovering, synthesizing, and analyzing information from a variety of sources, including tools, processes, documentation, and stakeholders.

Common activities include:

- Understanding enterprise problems and goals
- Analyzing needs and solutions
- Devising strategies
- Driving change
- Facilitating stakeholder collaboration

Common job titles for people performing business analysis: business architect, business systems analyst, data analyst, enterprise analyst, management consultant, process analyst, product manager, product owner, requirements engineer, and systems analyst.

## Structure of the BABOK® Guide

The BABOK® Guide is organized into several major components:

### Knowledge Areas (Core Content)

Knowledge areas are collections of logically (but not sequentially) related business analysis tasks. The **six knowledge areas** are:

1. **[[01_BA_Planning_and_Monitoring]]** — tasks to organize and coordinate the efforts of business analysts and stakeholders
2. **[[02_Elicitation_and_Collaboration]]** — tasks for preparing, conducting, and confirming elicitation activities and ongoing stakeholder collaboration
3. **[[03_Requirements_Life_Cycle_Management]]** — tasks to manage and maintain requirements and design information from inception to retirement
4. **[[04_Strategy_Analysis]]** — tasks to identify a business need of strategic or tactical importance and align the resulting strategy
5. **[[05_Requirements_Analysis_and_Design]]** — tasks to structure, specify, model, validate, and verify requirements and designs
6. **[[06_Solution_Evaluation]]** — tasks to assess the performance and value delivered by a solution in use

### Extended Content

- **Business Analysis Key Concepts** (this chapter): foundational definitions, the BACCM™, requirements classification schema, stakeholders, and requirements/design distinction
- **[[07_Underlying_Competencies]]**: behaviours, characteristics, knowledge, and personal qualities supporting effective practice
- **[[08_Techniques_Catalog]]**: means to perform business analysis tasks; covers the most common and widespread techniques
- **[[09_Perspectives]]**: various views of business analysis (Agile, Business Intelligence, Information Technology, Business Architecture, Business Process Management)

### Task Structure

Each task in the BABOK® Guide is presented in a consistent format: Purpose, Description, Inputs, Elements, Guidelines/Tools, Techniques, Stakeholders, and Outputs. Tasks may be performed in any order and from any starting point, as long as the necessary inputs are present. The Guide does not prescribe a process.

## The Business Analysis Core Concept Model™ (BACCM™)

The **BACCM™** is a conceptual framework for business analysis. It defines what business analysis is, independent of perspective, industry, methodology, or organizational level. It consists of **six core concepts** of equal importance, each defined by the other five. No single concept holds greater significance.

| Core Concept | Description |
|---|---|
| **Change** | The act of transformation in response to a need. Change works to improve the performance of an enterprise through deliberate and controlled business analysis activities. |
| **Need** | A problem or opportunity to be addressed. Needs can cause changes by motivating stakeholders to act. Changes can also cause needs by eroding or enhancing the value delivered by existing solutions. |
| **Solution** | A specific way of satisfying one or more needs in a context. A solution resolves a problem faced by stakeholders or enables stakeholders to take advantage of an opportunity. |
| **Stakeholder** | A group or individual with a relationship to the change, the need, or the solution. Stakeholders are defined in terms of interest in, impact on, and influence over the change. |
| **Value** | The worth, importance, or usefulness of something to a stakeholder within a context. Value can be tangible (directly measurable, often monetary) or intangible (measured indirectly, such as reputation or morale). Value can also decrease in the form of losses, risks, and costs. |
| **Context** | The circumstances that influence, are influenced by, and provide understanding of the change. Context includes attitudes, behaviours, beliefs, competitors, culture, demographics, goals, governments, infrastructure, languages, losses, processes, products, projects, sales, seasons, terminology, technology, weather, and any other relevant element. |

### Using the BACCM

The BACCM is used to:

- Describe the profession and domain of business analysis
- Communicate about business analysis with common terminology
- Evaluate relationships among key concepts
- Perform better business analysis by holistically evaluating relationships among all six concepts
- Evaluate the impact of concepts and relationships at any point during a work effort

When planning or performing a task, business analysts can ask:

- What are the kinds of **changes** we are doing?
- What are the **needs** we are trying to satisfy?
- What are the **solutions** we are creating or changing?
- Who are the **stakeholders** involved?
- What do stakeholders consider to be of **value**?
- What are the **contexts** that we and the solution are in?

If any core concept experiences a change, all concepts and their relationships to value delivery should be re-evaluated.

## Key Terms

The BABOK® Guide defines several foundational terms:

- **Business Analysis**: the practice of enabling change in an enterprise by defining needs and recommending solutions that deliver value to stakeholders.

- **Business Analysis Information**: the broad and diverse sets of information that business analysts analyze, transform, and report. Includes elicitation results, requirements, designs, solution options, solution scope, and change strategy. This term intentionally expands the scope beyond "requirements" to ensure that all inputs and outputs are subject to the Guide's tasks.

- **Design**: a usable representation of a solution. Design focuses on understanding *how* value might be realized by a solution if it is built.

- **Enterprise**: a system of one or more organizations and the solutions they use to pursue a shared set of common goals. Enterprise boundaries are defined relative to the change and need not be constrained by legal entity boundaries.

- **Organization**: an autonomous group of people under a single management, working towards common goals and objectives, with a clearly defined boundary and continuous operation.

- **Plan**: a proposal for doing or achieving something, describing a set of events, dependencies, expected sequence, schedule, results, materials, resources, and stakeholders.

- **Requirement**: a usable representation of a need. Requirements focus on understanding *what* kind of value could be delivered if a requirement is fulfilled.

- **Risk**: the effect of uncertainty on the value of a change, a solution, or the enterprise. Business analysts collaborate with stakeholders to identify, assess, prioritize, and deal with risks.

## Requirements Classification Schema

The BABOK® Guide classifies requirements into four categories, ordered by level of abstraction:

| Classification | Description |
|---|---|
| **Business Requirements** | Statements of goals, objectives, and outcomes that describe *why* a change has been initiated. They can apply to the whole enterprise, a business area, or a specific initiative. |
| **Stakeholder Requirements** | Describe the needs of stakeholders that must be met to achieve the business requirements. They serve as a bridge between business and solution requirements. |
| **Solution Requirements** | Describe the capabilities and qualities of a solution that meets stakeholder requirements. Divided into: (a) **functional requirements** — the behaviour and information the solution must manage; (b) **non-functional requirements** (quality of service) — conditions under which a solution must remain effective or qualities it must have. |
| **Transition Requirements** | Describe capabilities the solution must have and conditions it must meet to facilitate transition from the current state to the future state. They are temporary in nature and are not needed once the change is complete. Examples include data conversion, training, and business continuity. |

## Stakeholders

A stakeholder is any individual or group that a business analyst is likely to interact with directly or indirectly. Any stakeholder can be a source of requirements, assumptions, or constraints. A single individual may fill more than one role, and multiple stakeholder roles may exist within each category.

The BABOK® Guide defines the following generic stakeholder roles:

| Role | Description |
|---|---|
| **Business Analyst** | Responsible and accountable for executing business analysis activities. Inherently a stakeholder in all business analysis work. |
| **Customer** | Uses or may use products or services produced by the enterprise; may have contractual or moral rights. |
| **Domain Subject Matter Expert** | Any individual with in-depth knowledge of a topic relevant to the business need or solution scope (e.g., managers, process owners, legal staff, consultants). |
| **End User** | Stakeholders who directly interact with the solution. Includes all participants in a business process or who use the product or solution. |
| **Implementation Subject Matter Expert** | Has specialized knowledge regarding the implementation of one or more solution components (e.g., solution architect, developer, DBA, information architect, usability analyst, trainer, organizational change consultant). |
| **Operational Support** | Responsible for the day-to-day management and maintenance of a system or product (e.g., operations analyst, help desk, release manager). |
| **Project Manager** | Responsible for managing the work required to deliver a solution, balancing project factors including scope, budget, schedule, resources, quality, and risk. |
| **Regulator** | Responsible for the definition and enforcement of standards imposed through legislation, corporate governance, audit standards, or organizational centers of competency. |
| **Sponsor** | Responsible for initiating the effort to define a business need and develop a solution. Authorizes the work and controls the budget and scope. |
| **Supplier** | A stakeholder outside the organizational boundary providing products or services (e.g., providers, vendors, consultants). |
| **Tester** | Responsible for verifying that the solution meets requirements, ensuring conformance to quality standards, and minimizing risk of defects. |

## Requirements and Designs

Requirements and designs are closely related but have a distinct focus:

- **Requirements** are focused on the *need* (what kind of value could be delivered)
- **Designs** are focused on the *solution* (how value might be realized)

The distinction is not always clear-cut. The same techniques are used to elicit, model, and analyze both. A requirement leads to a design, which in turn may drive the discovery and analysis of more requirements — creating a cyclical, recursive relationship:

> **Business Requirements** → *Why do I want it?* → **Stakeholder Requirements** → *What are the needs?* → **Solution Requirements** → *What do I want?* → **Design** → (cycle continues until requirements are met) → **Transition Requirements** → *What are the conditions?*

Business analysis can be complex and recursive: a requirement may be used to define a design, which leads to more detailed requirements, which inform more detailed designs. Business analysts may hand off requirements and designs to other stakeholders for further elaboration, while retaining responsibility for reviewing final designs to ensure alignment.

### Examples of Requirements vs. Designs

| Requirement | Design |
|---|---|
| View six months sales data across multiple organizational units in a single view | A sketch of a dashboard |
| Reduce amount of time required to pick and pack a customer order | Process model |
| Record and access a medical patient's history | Screen mock-up showing specific data fields |
| Develop business strategy, goals, and objectives for a new business | Business Capability Model |
| Provide information in English and French | Prototype with text displayed in English and French |

The importance of the business analyst's role lies in continuously asking **"Why?"** — why is either the requirement or design necessary to provide value and facilitate the realization of enterprise goals and objectives.
