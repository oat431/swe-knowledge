# 04 Growing Productive Teams

> **Source:** DeMarco & Lister, *Peopleware* (3rd Ed.), Part IV, Chapters 21–28
> **Purpose:** Understand what makes teams jell, what kills them, and what managers can do to create the conditions where productive teams grow — because you can't *make* teams jell, but you can stop killing them.

---

## Chapter 21 — The Whole Is Greater Than the Sum of the Parts

### Why Teams Matter

Most work is done by individuals working alone — there is very little true teamwork required in knowledge work. Yet teams are still essential because:

> **The purpose of a team is not goal attainment but goal alignment.**

When the team fulfills its purpose, members become more effective because they're more *directed*. The team gets everyone pulling in the same direction.

### Corporate Goals vs. Team Goals

| Corporate Goals | Team Reality |
|-----------------|-------------|
| Profit, quarterly results, market share | Arbitrary and meaningless to individuals at the bottom |
| "Focus their efforts on Q2" | "Who gives a rat's ass for their second quarter?" |
| Abstract organizational objectives | The team itself, joint success, the pleasure of achieving *any* goal together |

The arbitrariness of goals doesn't prevent acceptance — sports proves this. People get involved because of the social units they belong to, not because the goal itself matters intrinsically. Jelled team members get so involved they'd storm the guns of Navarone just to pass a version 3 acceptance test.

### Signs of a Jelled Team

| Sign | Observed Behavior |
|------|------------------|
| **Low turnover** | Members won't leave until the work is done; money, status, advancement matter far less than before jell |
| **Strong identity** | Colorful team names (Okie Coders, Gang of Four, Chaos Group), shared catch phrases, in-jokes, obvious team space |
| **Sense of eliteness** | SWAT-team attitude, cocky, feel they're part of something unique |
| **Joint ownership** | Pleased to have names grouped together on a product; eager for peer review; workspace decorated with product views |
| **Genuine enjoyment** | Interactions are easy, confident, warm; the team feels healthy |

### Teams vs. Cliques

There is **no denotative difference** between a team and a clique — only connotation. "Team" = the tight bonding pleases you. "Clique" = it threatens you.

Fear of cliques is a sign of **managerial insecurity**. Insecure managers are threatened because:
- They are often not true members of their own teams
- Loyalties within the group are stronger than those tying the group to the company
- A tightly knit team might leave en masse

The insecure manager would prefer uniform plastic people — identical, interchangeable, unbonded. But the jelled group, for all its cockiness and exclusivity, serves the manager's real goals far better than any assemblage of interchangeable parts.

## Chapter 22 — The Black Team

The Black Team was a legendary testing group formed at a large computer company in the 1960s. They were initially just slightly better testers than their peers, free of the cognitive dissonance that hampers developers testing their own code. What happened next was unexpected.

### Evolution of Team Identity

| Phase | Behavior |
|-------|----------|
| **Early** | Finding bugs was a job — competent but unremarkable |
| **Adversary philosophy emerges** | They wanted and *expected* to find defects; they weren't rooting for developers |
| **Ordeal, not test** | Submitting a program felt like appearing before Ming the Merciless — monstrously unfair inputs, overloaded buffers, empty file comparisons |
| **Persona cultivation** | Dressed in black, cackled horribly at failures, twirled mustaches in Simon Legree fashion |
| **Self-reinforcing culture** | Gathered to design ever-more-awful testing ploys; programmers muttered about "diseased minds" |

### What Made It Work

The company was delighted — every defect the Black Team found was one the customers wouldn't. But more importantly, **the chemistry within the group had become an end in itself**. People got such a kick out of what they were doing that colleagues outside the team were positively jealous.

> The team survived the loss of **all** its original staff — replaced one by one over time — and emerged with its energy and personality intact. The culture outlived the individuals.

**Key Lesson:** Team culture, once formed, becomes self-sustaining. The silly exaggerated behavior (black outfits, cackling) was part of the fun, but the fundamental bonding around a shared adversarial identity was the real engine.

## Chapter 23 — Teamicide

DeMarco and Lister set out to write "Six Things You Can Do to Make Teams Jell." They got stuck at **zero**. Teams can't be *made* to jell — the process is too fragile to control. Pivoting, they asked: what makes team formation *impossible*? The answer came easily.

> **Teamicide** — the surefire ways to inhibit team formation and disrupt project sociology.

### The Seven Teamicides

| # | Teamicide | How It Kills Teams |
|---|-----------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Defensive management** | "You may operate autonomously, as long as you operate correctly" — amounts to no autonomy at all. Prescriptive methodologies, technical interference by the manager, everything must have the boss's approval. Message: *I don't trust you.* |
| 2 | **Bureaucracy** | Capers Jones found paperwork is the **second largest** category of development cost (>30%). Mindless paper pushing makes it impossible for workers to see themselves as SWAT-team members hell-bent for success. |
| 3 | **Physical separation** | Scattering teams across floors/buildings kills casual interaction, prevents group space and culture from forming. The Black Team couldn't exist if members weren't co-located — they'd be constantly interacting with people not in on the joke. |
| 4 | **Fragmentation of time** | People assigned to 4+ projects can't be part of any jelled team. The tight interactions of a jelled team are exclusive. Simple policy ("one project at a time") dramatically reduces fragmentation. |
| 5 | **Quality reduction** | Building a shoddy product undermines self-esteem and enjoyment. Co-workers developing a low-quality product don't want to look each other in the eye. No joint sense of accomplishment is possible. |
| 6 | **Phony deadlines** | When the boss announces an impossible deadline, everyone knows it. The message: "The boss is a Parkinsonian robot with no respect for us. We won't do a stroke of work except under duress." Don't expect a jelled team. |
| 7 | **Clique control** | Explicitly breaking up teams between projects, or missing every opportunity to keep them together. Management often only notices teams when taking steps to *break* them up. |

### Defensive Management in Detail

The most fundamental teamicide. The manager's defensive instinct makes sense for risks like failure-prone gear or vacillating clients. But **you can't protect yourself against your own people's incompetence.** Once you've chosen your team, the only winning tactic is to trust them.

> The right to be right (in your manager's eyes) is irrelevant; it's only the **right to be wrong** that makes you free.

Most managers err on the side of mistrust. They follow the premise that people may operate completely autonomously, **as long as they operate correctly**. This is no autonomy at all.

### The Bigger Pattern

Most organizations don't set out consciously to kill teams. They just act that way. And there are no jelled teams at the managerial level — the team phenomenon happens only at the bottom of the hierarchy. When managers are bonded into teams, it's only because they serve dual roles: manager *and* group member.

## Chapter 24 — Teamicide Revisited

Two additional forms of teamicide the authors missed in the first edition.

### Motivational Posters and Plaques

> "Quality Is Job One" — Oh. Gee, we never would have thought that. We sort of assumed — until this wonderful poster came along — that Quality was Job Twenty-nine.

These motivational accessories (posters, slogan coffee mugs, plaques, pins, awards) are a triumph of **form over substance**. They purport to extol Quality, Leadership, Creativity, Teamwork, Loyalty — but do so in such simplistic terms that the real message is:

**"Management here believes these virtues can be improved with posters rather than by hard work and managerial talent."**

Everyone quickly understands that the presence of posters is a sure sign of the *absence* of hard work and talent. A typical poster calls workers "common people" and "the pack." The only place where they do no harm is where people have ceased to register any further decline.

### Overtime as Teamicide

Extended overtime has an unanticipated teamicidal side effect: **it can never be applied uniformly to all team members.**

| Scenario | Effect |
|----------|--------|
| Allen (widower, primary caregiver) | Must pick up child at 5:15 daily; weekends are inviolable |
| Rest of team | Working 60+ hours/week for months; spouses, kids, laundry, bills all neglected |
| After months | "I'm getting pretty sick of carrying Allen" |
| Result | Team cohesion destroyed; the people not sharing the pain become estranged |

Members of good teams are never uniform — differing abilities to "borrow" time from personal lives mean overtime will inevitably create resentment. If overtime drags on over months, the team magic will be gone.

> Jerry Weinberg's insight: We don't work overtime so much to get the work done on time as to **shield ourselves from blame when the work inevitably doesn't get done on time.**

## Chapter 25 — Competition

### Internal Competition Kills Peer Coaching

In healthy teams, peer coaching is ubiquitous and barely even identified as coaching — it just seems like work. Team members sit in pairs: one learner, one teacher. Roles switch back and forth. This is how coordination and growth happen.

**Coaching cannot take place if people don't feel safe.** In a competitive atmosphere:
- Being coached signals you know less — dangerous
- Coaching someone else arms them to pass you by — also dangerous

### Managerial Actions That Foster Competition (and Are Therefore Teamicidal)

- Annual salary or merit reviews
- Management by objectives (MBO)
- Praise of certain workers for extraordinary accomplishment
- Awards, prizes, bonuses tied to performance
- Performance measurement in almost any form

### Deming's Point 12B

W. Edwards Deming's Fourteen Points include:

> Remove barriers that rob people in management and in engineering of their right to pride of workmanship. This means abolishment of the annual or merit rating and of management by objectives.

Deming's argument: MBO and its ilk are **managerial cop-outs**. By using simplistic extrinsic motivators to goad performance, managers excuse themselves from harder matters — investment, direct personal motivation, thoughtful team formation, staff retention, and ongoing work procedure redesign.

### The Sports Metaphor Problem

The authors confess growing disenchantment with the sports-team metaphor. Problems:

| Sports Team | Jelled Work Group |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Internal competition is rampant ("riding the pines") | Competition inhibits jell |
| Individual success can happen while team fails | Individual and team success are inseparable |
| Individual failure can happen while team succeeds | Members share the outcome |

> A **choir or glee club** makes a far better metaphor: you'll never have people congratulate you on singing your part perfectly while the choir as a whole sings off-key. The success of the individual is tied irrevocably to the success of the whole.

## Chapter 26 — A Spaghetti Dinner

### The Story

A manager invites her new project team to her house for dinner. When everyone arrives, there's no meal prepared. She suggests they walk to a nearby supermarket and "put a spaghetti dinner together." Nobody takes charge — but salads get assembled, clam sauce gets discussed, garlic bread gets volunteered, wine gets picked. Back at the house, dinner gradually comes together with no formal direction.

### What Was Really Happening

The team had their **first joint success** — before billing a single day to the project. Success breeds success; productive harmony breeds more productive harmony.

> Good managers provide frequent, easy opportunities for the team to succeed together. The opportunities may be tiny pilot subprojects, demonstrations, simulations — anything that gets the team quickly into the habit of succeeding together.

### The Natural Manager

The best success is the one in which there is **no evident management** — the team works as a genial aggregation of peers. The best boss manages this over and over without team members knowing they've been "managed."

These managers are viewed by peers as just lucky. Everything breaks right for them. They never break into a sweat. It looks so easy that no one believes they are managing at all.

**Key Pattern:** The manager's hand is organized for small, easy, joint successes. You have to look twice to see it — it just seems to be happening.

## Chapter 27 — Open Kimono

Open Kimono management is the exact opposite of defensive management. You take **no steps to defend yourself** from the people you've put into positions of trust. And all the people under you are in positions of trust.

### Calling In Well

People talk about organizations you'd have to be "sick" to work for — meaning the job would require disregarding mental survival rules that protect self-regard. A job that hurts your self-regard is itself "sick."

> Calling in well: "Listen, I've been sick ever since I started working here, but today I'm well and I won't be in anymore."

Managers of well workers:
- Respect autonomy once granted
- Accept that a worker's failure will reflect badly on them — that's just the breaks
- Put the right person in the position, then don't second-guess
- Know that a person they can't trust with autonomy is of no use to them

### The Getaway Ploy

The most common form of defensive management is direct physical oversight — wandering through work areas, the "Parkinsonian Patrol." The counter-technique:

> Send them away. Find a remote office, hire a conference room, borrow a summer house, put them up at a hotel. Tell them, "Come back when you're done."

The manager stays behind to fend off interruptions. The team works with total autonomy. Results are judged by the product they bring back, not by visual supervision.

> Visual supervision is a joke for development workers. Visual supervision is for prisoners.

This costs points with upper management ("How do you know they aren't loafing?"), but it removes people from distractions, gives them autonomy, and improves the chance of jelling.

### Skunkworks and Sensible Insubordination

The skunkworks project — building something management has killed — is really just insubordination. One company president awarded the team a medal: **"First Annual Prize for Insubordination"** — with the speech that others seeking the award had better be just as successful.

People look out for their Open Kimono managers. They're determined to make them look good, even if the manager botches an occasional decision. Defensive managers are on their own.

### Natural Authority

Between master craftsman and apprentice there is a bond of natural authority that demeans no one. An insecure need for obedience is the opposite:

> "Recognize me as a different caste of creature, a manager. I belong to the thinking class. Those beneath me are employed to carry out my decisions."

In the best organizations, natural authority works in all directions — the manager is trusted for direction, negotiation, and hiring; each worker is trusted as a natural authority in their specialty.

## Chapter 28 — Chemistry for Team Formation

Some organizations are famous for consistent good luck in getting well-knit teams. It isn't luck — it's **chemistry**. Departments that glow with health do so because their managers make it happen.

### The Six Elements of a Chemistry-Building Strategy

| # | Element | How It Works |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | **Make a cult of quality** | "Only perfect is good enough for us" — the strongest catalyst. Binds the team because it sets members apart from the rest of the world. The marketplace never makes the case for quality; the team must be protected from short-term economics. |
| 2 | **Provide lots of satisfying closure** | Humans need reassurance. The organization may need closure only at the end of a four-year effort, but people need frequent "thunks" of pieces falling into place. Deliver in 20 versions even if 2 are sufficient for management. |
| 3 | **Build a sense of eliteness** | People require a sense of uniqueness to be at peace with themselves. When uniqueness is stifled, it happens anyway — often in perverse forms (pride in being difficult to manage). A team's eliteness threatens managerial trappings, not manageability. |
| 4 | **Allow and encourage heterogeneity** | A little heterogeneity is an enormous aid to jelling. Adding one handicapped developer, a co-op student, or a retrained ex-admin signals "it's okay not to be a clone." The saddest example is the all-male team. |
| 5 | **Preserve and protect successful teams** | Don't break up the Yankees. If a team knits, give them the option to undertake another project together. They start with enormous momentum. |
| 6 | **Provide strategic but not tactical direction** | Set the direction, then get out of the way. The team assigns itself the task of setting and upholding a standard of prideful workmanship. |

### The Cult of Quality

> A salesman was contemptuous of a German-made shredder that cost half again as much without a single extra feature: "All you get for that extra money is better quality."

Your marketplace, clients, and upper management will never make the case for high quality — it doesn't make good short-term economic sense. When team members develop a cult of quality, they always turn out something better than what the market asks for. They get high on quality and outdo themselves to protect it.

### On Not Breaking Up the Yankees

If a team knits, **don't break it up**. At minimum, give people the option to stay together on the next project. When teams stay together from one project to the next, they start with enormous momentum.

### The Network Model of Team Behavior

> Managers are usually **not part of the teams they manage.**

| Traditional View (Hierarchy) | Reality (Network) |
|------------------------------|-------------------|
| Manager = quarterback, permanent leader | Different individuals provide occasional leadership in their strength areas |
| Top-down direction | Peers functioning as equals |
| Manager inside the team | Manager outside, clearing obstacles and giving occasional direction |

On the best teams, no one is the permanent leader — because that person would cease to be a peer and team interaction would break down. The structure of a team is a **network, not a hierarchy**.

### Putting It All Together

You can't always make it happen, but when a team comes together, it's worth the cost:

- Work is fun; people are energized
- They roll over deadlines and look for more
- They like themselves
- They feel loyal to the team and to the environment that allows the team to exist

## Key Takeaways (Chapters 21–28)

1. **Teams can't be made to jell — they can only be grown.** Agriculture is the right metaphor: you enrich the soil, plant seeds, water, and hold your breath. You might get a crop; you might not.
2. **The purpose of a team is goal alignment, not goal attainment.** Most work is done alone; the team ensures everyone pulls in the same direction.
3. **Trust is the foundation.** Defensive management is the primary teamicide. The right to be wrong is what makes people free. Open Kimono — trusting people completely — gives teams their best chance.
4. **Internal competition kills coaching.** Merit reviews, MBO, performance bonuses — all the standard managerial tools — are teamicidal. The choir, not the sports team, is the better metaphor.
5. **Provide easy joint successes.** The spaghetti dinner principle: small, low-stakes opportunities to succeed together build the habit of winning as a team.
6. **Quality is the strongest catalyst.** The cult of quality binds teams because it sets them apart. Protect teams from short-term economics so they can deliver better than the market asks for.
7. **Frequent closure matters.** The organization may only need two deliveries; the team needs twenty. Each closure is a "thunk" that reinforces the team bond.
8. **Preserve teams that work.** Don't break up the Yankees. Let successful teams choose to stay together.
9. **Managers are not team members.** The team is a network of peers. The manager clears obstacles and sets strategic direction from outside.
10. **Chemistry is the manager's real job.** In the healthiest organizations, managers devote their energy to building and maintaining healthy chemistry — and everything else follows.

## Teamicide Quick-Reference

| # | Teamicide | Antidote |
|---|-----------|----------|
| 1 | Defensive management | Trust your people; let them make mistakes |
| 2 | Bureaucracy | Minimize mindless paper pushing |
| 3 | Physical separation | Co-locate teams; give them shared space |
| 4 | Fragmentation of time | One project per person at a time |
| 5 | Quality reduction | Protect the cult of quality |
| 6 | Phony deadlines | Don't lie about deadlines |
| 7 | Clique control | Let teams stay together |
