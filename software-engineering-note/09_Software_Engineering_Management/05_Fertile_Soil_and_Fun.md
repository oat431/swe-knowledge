# Fertile Soil and Fun

> **Source:** DeMarco & Lister, *Peopleware: Productive Projects and Teams* (3rd ed.), Parts V–VI, Chapters 29–39
> **Theme:** The culture that enables great teams — self-healing systems, risk, meetings, e-mail, change, learning, community, chaos, free electrons, and waking the sleeping giant.

## Part V: Fertile Soil

### 29. The Self-Healing System

Human systems are **nondeterministic** — they can fix gaps left by their designers on the fly. When you automate or impose rigid process, the system becomes **deterministic** and loses this self-healing capacity.

#### Big-M Methodology vs. Small-m methodology

| | Big-M **Methodology** | Small-m **methodology** |
|---|---|---|
| Where it lives | Fat book on a shelf | Inside people's heads |
| Who thinks | The authors | The workers |
| Nature | Centralized, imposed | Tailored, owned |

The **covert meaning** of imposing a Methodology: *"Project people aren't smart enough to do the thinking."*

**Four damages of Big-M Methodology:**
1. **Morass of paperwork** — "Voluminous documentation is part of the problem, not part of the solution."
2. **Paucity of methods** — standardizing on one excludes all alternatives; knowledge used sparingly.
3. **Absence of responsibility** — when the Methodology made all decisions, no one is accountable.
4. **General loss of motivation** — nothing is more demotivating than management thinking workers incompetent.

**Malicious compliance:** Workers follow the Methodology to the letter even when they know it will produce waste — the white-collar equivalent of *work to rule*.

#### Better Ways to Achieve Convergence of Method

Instead of imposing Methodology by statute:
- **Training** — people use what they know
- **Tools** — automated aids produce more convergence than all the statutes
- **Peer review** — quality circles, walkthroughs, inspections create natural convergence

**DuPont's standard:** *"A proven method for undertaking a repeated task"* — proven means "demonstrated widely and successfully within DuPont." Standardize only what is already de facto standard.

#### The Hawthorne Effect

At Hawthorne Western Electric (1932), changing light levels up *or* down increased productivity. The change itself mattered, not the direction. People perform better when trying something new.

> Let a hundred flowers blossom and let a hundred schools of thought contend.
> — Mao Tse-tung (but DeMarco & Lister actually mean it)

**Rule of thumb:** All standards imposed on your people should fit in **no more than ten pages**. Grant exceptions freely. Run every project as a pilot.

### 30. Dancing with Risk

> Project risk is a good thing, a likely indicator of value. Projects that have real value but little or no risk were all done ages ago.

**Barnes & Noble Nook example:** Took a huge risk on library e-book borrowing to differentiate from Amazon's Kindle. The risk paid off.

#### The One Risk We Almost Never Manage

**The risk of our own failure.** It feels like defeatism to list it. But risk management exists not to make risks disappear — it exists to enable **sensible mitigation** should they occur.

**Denver International Airport Baggage Handling System:** Management declared lateness "not a risk" by fiat. When the system was late, there was no backup — the airport opening was deferred by over a year, costing billions.

> It's perfectly reasonable not to manage a risk with extremely low probability. It's *not* reasonable to leave unmanaged the risk whose consequences are "just too awful to think about."

#### False Challenge Projects

When executives characterize a desired outcome as a *challenge*, they're often not goading excellence but trying to deliver **on the cheap**. The real meaning of "We must have this by January 1st" is often: *"This work is so unimportant that we don't want to fund it beyond January 1st."*

**False challenge pattern:** marginal benefit (no real technological risk taken) + huge schedule risk (typically unmanaged). Welcome to the worst of both worlds.

### 31. Meetings, Monologues, and Conversations

#### Neuro-sclerosis

As organizations age, meeting time increases until there's time for nothing else. Reasons:
- More vested parties per decision → larger meetings
- Meetings give visibility to those who want to rise
- Competitive windbagging disguised as "monumental complexity"

#### The "Technologically Enhanced" Meeting

Laptops in meetings don't facilitate the meeting — they provide **escape** from its pointlessness. Technology has made meetings *worse*: a generation ago, people would have revolted; today, the laptops make bad meetings bearable.

**Stand-up meetings:** No surface for a laptop, so they stay closed. Meetings tend to be shorter.

#### Working Meetings vs. Ceremonies

| Working Meeting | Ceremony |
|---|---|
| Called to **reach a decision** | Called for FYI, ritual |
| Ends when decision is reached | **Ends when the clock says so** |
| Attendance: only those who must agree | Attendance: the more the merrier |
| Has a clear termination condition | No specific group action ends it |

**Test:** "What group action terminates this meeting?" If you can't answer, it's a ceremony.

#### Prescriptions

- Eliminate most ceremonial meetings; replace with one-on-one conversations
- Limit working meeting attendance to vested-interest participants only
- Apply the "What ends this meeting?" test
- Encourage **Open-Space networking** (all coffee break, no formal sessions)
- Release at least one person at the start of each meeting — the message is hard to miss

### 32. The Ultimate Management Sin Is...

> **Wasting people's time.**

#### Status Meetings Are About Status

Not the status of the work — the **status of the boss**. The ceremony supplies reassurance. The boss's real need being served is not for information but for **reassurance** and confirmation of hierarchy.

#### Early Overstaffing

The ideal staffing curve puts a small team on planning/design for up to half the project duration, then ramps up. But when deadlines truncate the timeline, managers pad the *beginning* instead. Result: people brought on too early, with nothing productive to do.

> Probably no more than 90 percent of all projects suffer from early overstaffing.

It is **politically unsafe** to run a project leanly through analysis and design — even though that's the right thing to do.

#### Fragmentation

Assigning one person to multiple tasks fragments their time and kills flow. The waste is **invisible** — it's the slow restart after each interruption. Particularly injurious when tasks require qualitatively different work habits (e.g., deep design vs. interrupt-driven support).

> The human capital invested in your workforce represents a ton of money. Wasting the time of that huge investment is money poured down the drain.

### 33. E(vil) Mail

E-mail reduced coordination cost by 100× and speed by 1000×. Did we pocket the savings? No — we plowed them into **hundreds of times more coordination**.

**Over-coordination:** When you over-coordinate your people, they under-coordinate themselves. The manager's job is not to coordinate interaction — it's to help people learn to **self-coordinate**.

#### Corporate Spam

Most spam in your inbox comes from **co-workers**. Any message sent to one person with CCs to half a dozen others is candidate spam.

**The "need-to-know" test:** For each message, ask: *"Do I need to know this?"*

**Hidden motives behind CCs:**
- "If I don't send this around, how will anyone know I'm working?"
- "I don't dare not send this — people will complain."
- "This is an open organization, so everybody has to see everything."
- "I want this widely seen because it shows what a great writer I am."

#### Repeal Passive Consent

The unwritten rule: **Silence gives consent.** If someone CCs you on a proposal and you don't object, you've consented. This forces everyone to read everything. Repeal it — establish that only **explicit consent** gives consent.

### 34. Making Change Possible

> "People hate change. They really, really do."
> — Steve McMenamin

**Machiavelli (1513):** *"The introducer has all those who benefit from the old orders as enemies, and he has lukewarm defenders in all those who might benefit from the new orders."*

#### The Resistance-to-Change Continuum (Jerry Johnson)

```
Blindly Loyal ── Believers But Questioners ── Militantly Opposed
   (enemy)       (only meaningful allies)        (enemy)
```

- **Blindly Loyal:** Will hop onto whatever appears hot, then abandon it for the next fad. *Not allies.*
- **Believers But Questioners:** Skeptics ("Show me"), passive observers ("What's in it for me?"), those who fear change or loss of power. **The only meaningful potential allies.**
- **Militantly Opposed:** Will undermine and destroy. *Not allies.*

> **MANTRA:** The fundamental response to change is not logical, but emotional.

Never demean the old ways. **Celebrate the old** to help change happen. Example: *"The CGS system has been running for 14 years, handling over 1,000,000 take-offs and landings. Now we have the chance to redesign it, and we need your expertise."*

#### The Satir Change Model

```
Old Status Quo → [Foreign Element] → Chaos → [Transforming Idea] → Practice & Integration → New Status Quo
```

- **Chaos is integral to change.** Without expecting it, people mistake Chaos for the New Status Quo and change back.
- **The more painful the Chaos, the greater the perceived value of the New Status Quo** — if you get there.
- **Safety first:** Change only has a chance if failure — at least a little bit of failure — is also okay. Adults fear humiliation more than injury; kids don't.
- **Temporary loss of mastery** is embarrassing enough without catching flack for it.

### 35. Organizational Learning

> Experience is not the same as learning. The French army had centuries of experience guarding its eastern borders and still built the Maginot Line.

**Experience becomes learning only when an organization alters itself** in response to what experience has shown:
1. Instilling **new skills and approaches** in people (human capital)
2. **Redesigning** itself to operate differently

> **Learning is limited by an organization's ability to keep its people.** When turnover is high, learning can't stick.

#### Where Does Learning Happen?

- **Not at the top** — executives are focused on acquisitions, not day-to-day operations
- **Not at the bottom** — too constrained by organizational boundaries, lack power to effect change
- **In the middle** — middle management is the natural learning center

**Downsizings almost always target middle management** — which means they often destroy the learning center.

#### The Management Team Is Rarely a Team

Most "management teams" are collections of individuals with no common goals, values, or blended skills. They suffer every teamicidal effect (defensiveness, bureaucracy, fragmentation, physical separation, competition) and lack the one thing any team needs to jell: **common ownership of the work product.**

> **Danger in the White Space:** The learning center lies in the white space between middle managers. If that space is vital with communication and shared stake, learning prospers. If it's empty — isolation, fear, embattlement — learning stops.

### 36. The Making of Community

The need for community is built into human firmware. Most of us no longer find it in our towns — the workplace is our best chance.

#### Aristotelian Politics (The Noble Science)

Aristotle's five interlinked Noble Sciences: Metaphysics → Logic → Ethics → **Politics** → Aesthetics. Politics is the science of extending Ethics to the larger group, of creating and managing communities consistent with ethical behavior. **This is the key practice of good management.** Refusing to be political in the Aristotelian sense is an abnegation of managerial responsibility.

#### Why Community Matters

- Strong community → people don't want to leave → **turnover drops**
- When people do leave, they **time departures to project boundaries** — worth more than all process improvement over a decade
- **Deathbed assessment:** Building a real community is the kind of accomplishment that matters at the end of a life. It's a creation. It's Art.

#### How to Build Community

No formula. It requires talent, courage, creativity, and enormous time investment. You are the catalyst, not the sole builder.

**Example:** A company built a day-care and school for employees' children. Every afternoon, teachers lead a noisy parade of kids through the facility. All work pauses. Everyone feels great. Imagine having created that.

## Part VI: It's Supposed to Be Fun to Work Here

### 37. Chaos and Order

We are implacable enemies of chaos. But chaos is a **precious commodity** — without it, we'd be bored to tears. Managers tend to hog all the chaos; the Open Kimono manager parcels it out so others get the fun of putting things shipshape.

#### Constructive Reintroduction of Small Amounts of Disorder

- **Pilot projects** — try one new technique per project; don't experiment with everything at once. "It makes far more sense to run all projects as pilots than to run no projects as pilots."
- **War games** — competitive, no-lose, team-based tournaments over a weekend. Cater good food, work through the night, celebrate all accomplishments. People will say it was the most exciting experience of their career.
- **Brainstorming** — structured sessions targeting creative insight. Quantity over quality, no evaluation during the session, keep it loose and silly. Use analogy, inversion, and immersion as restart ploys.
- **Training, trips, conferences, celebrations, and retreats** — getting people out of the office together. Even a $100 hot-dog-vendor lunch on the 30th floor can become legend.

### 38. Free Electrons

The cottage-industry phenomenon: many of the best people are self-employed, contracting, freelancing. They have more freedom, more choice, more fun — and often more money.

#### Creating In-House Alternatives

Organizations create "free electron" positions with loosely stated responsibilities:
- "Investigate new methods for the twenty-first century"
- "Put together a new and exciting training sequence"
- "Define your own job"

These positions work because **companies profit from them** — free electrons contribute disproportionately. They are motivated to make their positions pay off.

> "Upper management has bought into a person, rather than into a concept. The person then defines and sells the concept."
> — Richard Branton

> "It works when people are self-motivated, and when they let reality dictate their directions."
> — Bill Bonham

#### Managing Free Electrons

Most people need a well-defined charter. But the mark of the **best manager** is the ability to single out the few who don't — and then **get out of their way**. Their own direction is more unerringly in the organization's best interest than any direction from above.

### 39. Holgar Dansk

> El Cordobes, asked about his exercise routine: *"There is something you don't understand, my friend. I don't wrestle the bull."*

You don't need to wrestle the bull alone. **One change is plenty.** Even a single substantive change to the sociology of your organization is a mammoth accomplishment.

#### The Sleeping Giant

Holgar Dansk is the legendary sleeping giant of Denmark — he awakens when the country is in danger. **There is a Holgar Dansk inside your organization:** the body of co-workers and subordinates, rational people whose patience is nearly exhausted. They know *Silly* when they see it.

**It doesn't take much to wake the giant.** One small voice saying, "This is unacceptable." Once said out loud, people can't ignore it.

**Examples of Holgar waking up:**
- A government agency stuffed telephone bells with tissues — now only a gentle purr of clicks
- A California company's paging-system wires keep getting cut in the programmer area
- A Minneapolis manager refused to move his people to smaller, noisier quarters — he had assembled evidence that they couldn't do work there, and Holgar was on his side
- An Australian company lets individuals form their own teams rather than assigning them

> Sociology matters more than technology or even money. It's supposed to be productive, satisfying fun to work. If it isn't, then there's nothing else worth concentrating on.

**Choose your terrain carefully, assemble your facts, and speak up.** You can make a difference — with a little help from Holgar Dansk.

## Key Takeaways

| Area | Principle |
|---|---|
| **Methodology** | Small-m methodology in people's heads > Big-M Methodology in fat books. Standards should describe what already works, not impose untested theory. |
| **Risk** | Manage the risk of your own failure. False challenges with managed schedule risk are better than unmanaged desperation. |
| **Meetings** | Working meetings end when a decision is made; ceremonies end when the clock says so. Kill the ceremonies. |
| **Time** | Wasting people's time is the ultimate management sin. Fragmentation is invisible waste. |
| **E-mail** | Corporate spam and "silence gives consent" are organizational dysfunction. Coach self-coordination, don't over-coordinate. |
| **Change** | People resist change emotionally, not logically. Chaos is a necessary stage — don't mistake it for failure. Safety is prerequisite. |
| **Learning** | Learning lives in middle management. Downsizing destroys it. The management team must become a real team. |
| **Community** | Community reduces turnover and gives life meaning. Aristotelian Politics is the manager's real job. |
| **Chaos** | Constructively reintroduce small amounts of disorder — pilot projects, war games, brainstorming, celebrations. |
| **Free Electrons** | Your best people may need no charter at all. Get out of their way. |
| **Holgar Dansk** | Don't wrestle the bull. Wake the sleeping giant — one small voice is often enough. |
