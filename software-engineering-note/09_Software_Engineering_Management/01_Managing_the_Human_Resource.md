# Managing the Human Resource

> *"The manager's function is not to make people work, but to make it possible for people to work."* — Sharon Weinberg

---

## Chapter 1: Somewhere Today, a Project Is Failing

### The High-Tech Illusion

Most of us are **not** in a high-tech business — we are **appliers** of technology, not its inventors. The researchers who made fundamental breakthroughs are in high-tech; the rest of us use their work to build products.

Because we work in teams and tightly knit working groups, **we are mostly in the human communication business**. Successes stem from good human interactions; failures stem from poor ones.

We focus on the technical side not because it's more crucial, but because it's **easier**. Installing a disk drive is trivial compared to figuring out why a team member is demotivated. Human interactions are messy but matter more than any other aspect of the work.

> *"If you find yourself concentrating on the technology rather than the sociology, you're like the vaudeville character who loses his keys on a dark street and looks for them on the adjacent street because, as he explains, 'The light is better there.'"*

## Chapter 2: Make a Cheeseburger, Sell a Cheeseburger

Development is **inherently different from production**, yet many managers apply a production-oriented management philosophy.

### Production Mindset (Wrong for Development)

- Squeeze out error — make the human machine run smoothly
- Take a hard line about people goofing off
- Treat workers as **interchangeable** pieces
- Optimize the steady state
- Standardize procedure — do everything by the book
- Eliminate experimentation — "headquarters does the thinking"

### The Right Approach for Thinking Workers

#### A Quota for Errors
Mistakes are a natural, healthy part of knowledge work. Fostering an atmosphere that doesn't allow for error makes people **defensive** — they stop trying things that might fail. Ask your people what dead-end roads they've explored. When people blow it, they should be congratulated — that's part of what they're being paid for.

#### Management Is Not "Kicking Ass"
The "Bozo definition" of management (management = kicking ass) treats workers as mindless executors. You can kick people to make them *active*, but not *creative, inventive, and thoughtful*. Most people already love their work — Draconian measures are usually superfluous.

#### There Is No "People Store"
In production, workers are interchangeable parts. Development managers who adopt this view fear acknowledging that anyone is irreplaceable. But uniqueness is what makes project chemistry vital — it's something to be **cultivated**, not suppressed.

> The "food criminal" anecdote: a manager branded a talented employee a "food criminal" because he spent 50% more on food (but saved on everything else — total cost was the same). He wasn't more expensive, he was just **different**.

#### A Project in Steady State Is Dead
A project's entire purpose is to **put itself out of business**. The only steady state in project life is *rigor mortis*. We assess people by steady-state metrics (lines of code, pages of documentation) but pay too little attention to how each person fits into the **dynamic** whole.

> **The Catalyst**: A woman who was "not a great developer or tester" had never worked on a project that wasn't a huge success over 12 years. She helped teams **jell** — she made communication work and projects fun. A catalyst is worth two people who just do work.

#### We Haven't Got Time to Think, Only to Do
Not 100% of time should be spent "doing." There must be provision for: brainstorming, investigating new methods, figuring out how to avoid sub-tasks, reading, training, and just goofing off.

> The average software developer doesn't own a single book on their work and hasn't ever read one.

We spend a scant **5%** of our time on planning, investigating new methods, training, reading, estimating, budgeting, scheduling, and allocating personnel.

## Chapter 3: Vienna Waits for You

### Spanish Theory Management

| Spanish Theory | English Theory |
|:--|:--|
| Fixed amount of value on earth | Value can be **created** through ingenuity and technology |
| Path to wealth = extract more efficiently from soil or people's backs | Led to the Industrial Revolution |
| Managers who count a 90-hour week as 40 hours | True productivity = achieving more **per hour** of work |

Spanish Theory managers dream of productivity through **unpaid overtime**. They bully people into long hours, trick them into hopeless schedules, and shame them into sacrificing everything for arbitrary deadlines.

> *"When will you realize… Vienna waits for you?"* — Billy Joel, "The Stranger"

### There Ain't No Such Thing as Overtime

Overtime for salaried workers is a **figment of the naïve manager's imagination**. A few extra hours on Saturday to meet a Monday deadline are almost always followed by an equal period of compensatory **"undertime"** — time on the phone, in bull sessions, or resting. Nobody can really work more than ~40 hours continually with the intensity required for creative intellectual work.

> Overtime is like sprinting: it makes sense for the last hundred yards of the marathon, but if you start sprinting in the first mile, you're just wasting time.

### Workaholics

Workaholics *will* put in uncompensated overtime — but only for a while. Once the realization hits that they've sacrificed family, love, home, and youth for work, the worker is **lost forever**. They don't calmly explain things need to change — they just quit (burnout).

**Workaholism is like the common cold** — everyone has a bout now and then. If you exploit workaholics in typical Spanish Theory fashion, you'll eventually lose them. The loss of a good person isn't worth it.

### Productivity: Winning Battles and Losing Wars

Productivity discussions almost **never** mention employee turnover. Typical "productivity improvement" measures:

- Pressure people to put in more hours
- Mechanize the development process
- Compromise product quality
- Standardize procedures

These make work *less enjoyable* and risk driving key people away. Productivity must be defined as:

$$\text{Productivity} = \frac{\text{Benefit (savings + revenue)}}{\text{Total Cost (including replacement of workers used up)}}$$

> The Eagle project at Data General: a Spanish Theory triumph. Workaholic team members put in endless unpaid overtime. At the end, virtually the **entire development staff quit**. What was the cost? No one even figured it into the equation.

> A project manager was on schedule and guaranteed delivery by March 1. Upper management's response: move the deadline to **January 15**. A schedule the project could actually meet was of no value — it didn't put people under pressure.

> *"People under time pressure don't work better — they just work faster."*

## Chapter 4: Quality — If Time Permits

### Self-Esteem and Quality

In the workplace, the **major arouser of emotions is threatened self-esteem**. We tie our self-esteem strongly to the **quality** of the product we produce — not the quantity. Any step that jeopardizes quality will set your staff's emotions directly against you.

### The Flight from Excellence

Managers jeopardize quality by setting unreachable deadlines. Under extreme time pressure, workers will:

- Push problems under the rug
- Foist issues onto the end user
- Deliver unstable, incomplete products
- **Hate** what they're doing

> The builders' minimum quality standard is more or less the **best quality they have achieved in the past** — invariably higher than what the market requires.

The market's true quality standard: software users regularly accept applications with **1–3 defects per 100 lines of code**. He who pays the piper is calling for a low-quality tune.

### Quality Is Free, But Only If You Pay Heavily for It

The common misinterpretation: managers hear "Quality Is Free" (Crosby) and think they can have unlimited free quality without investment. The real message:

> **Quality is free, but only to those who are willing to pay heavily for it.**

A policy of *"Quality — If Time Permits"* assures that **no quality at all** sneaks into the product.

Hewlett-Packard made a cult of quality. The result: developers know they're part of a culture that delivers quality beyond market requirements, leading to increased job satisfaction and **some of the lowest turnover in the industry**.

> Japan: the nation famous for high quality is also famous for high productivity. *"The trade-off between price and quality does not exist in Japan. Rather, the idea that high quality brings on cost reduction is widely accepted."* — Tajima & Matsubara

### Power of Veto

At Hitachi Software and Fujitsu, project teams have **effective power of veto** over delivery of a not-yet-ready product. Even if the client would accept a substandard product, the team can insist delivery wait until their own standards are met. This requires a deeply ingrained quality culture — and nerves of steel from management.

## Chapter 5: Parkinson's Law Revisited

### Parkinson's Law Is Not a Law

C. Northcote Parkinson was a **humorist**, not a scientist. His "law" (work expands to fill the time allocated) caught on because it was **funny**, not because it was true. He collected no data; he observed a fictitious government bureaucracy (possibly patterned on the British Post Office).

> **Parkinson's Law almost certainly doesn't apply to your people.**

Knowledge workers enjoy their work. They are disinclined to let it drag on forever — that would just delay the satisfaction they hanker for. They are as eager as you are to get the job done, provided they don't have to compromise quality.

### When Someone Isn't Performing

Reasons people don't perform in a healthy environment:
1. **Lack of competence**
2. **Lack of confidence**
3. **Lack of affiliation** with others on the project and its goals

Schedule pressure doesn't help any of these. The overwhelmed worker doesn't need more pressure — he needs **reassignment**, possibly to another company.

### The UNSW Data (Lawrence & Jeffery, 1985)

Productivity by estimation approach (103 projects):

| Estimation Approach | Avg Productivity | N |
|:--|:--:|:--:|
| Programmer alone | 8.0 | 19 |
| Supervisor alone | 6.6 | 23 |
| Programmer & supervisor | 7.8 | 16 |
| Systems analyst | 9.5 | 21 |
| **No estimate** | **12.0** | **24** |

> Projects with **no schedule pressure at all** ("Just wake me up when you're done") had the **highest productivity**.

Bad, hopelessly tight estimates sap builders' energy. When the schedule is "totally unreasonable and unrealistic," morale drops to the bottom — regardless of who set the estimate.

### Organizational Parkinsonism

> *Organizational busy work tends to expand to fill the working day.*

This is the *company* exhibiting Parkinsonian behavior, not its employees.

## Chapter 6: Laetrile

### The Seven False Hopes (Sirens) of Software Management

Like terminal cancer patients buying laetrile (apricot pit extract) because no one has anything better to offer, desperate managers are easy victims of productivity panaceas.

| False Hope | Reality |
|:--|:--|
| **1. There's some new trick you've missed** | You're not dumb enough to have missed something fundamental. This is a fear tactic by those selling it. |
| **2. Other managers are getting 100–200% gains** | Even if coding and testing were eliminated entirely, you couldn't get 100% gain. Analysis, negotiation, training, and cutover remain. |
| **3. Technology is passing you by** | Most of what we do is not truly high-tech. Requirements and specification (the low-tech part) still dominate. Software productivity improves only 3–5% per year. |
| **4. Changing languages will give huge gains** | Languages affect only the implementation part. At best, a ~5% gain. |
| **5. You need to double productivity immediately (the backlog)** | The software backlog is a myth. Projects stuck there are economic losers that wouldn't be built if we knew their real cost. |
| **6. Automate away your development staff** | Developers' principal work is **human communication** to organize user needs into formal procedure. That's not automatable. |
| **7. People work better under pressure** | They won't — they'll just enjoy it less. |

### This Is Management

> Sharon Weinberg found Tom DeMarco sick at the console before a user demo. She brought him soup and buoyed his spirits. When he asked how she found time for such things, she said: *"Tom, this is management."*

The manager's function is **not to make people work, but to make it possible for people to work.**

## Key Takeaways

1. **We're in the human communication business**, not high-tech. Sociology matters more than technology.
2. **Development ≠ production.** Don't manage knowledge workers like a fast-food franchise.
3. **Mistakes are healthy.** Create an environment where errors are expected and even celebrated.
4. **Uniqueness is an asset.** There is no "People Store" — cultivate individuality, don't suppress it.
5. **A project in steady state is dead.** Value catalysts and team dynamics, not just individual output.
6. **Overtime is a myth.** Compensatory undertime cancels it out. Sprinting in the first mile wastes time.
7. **Spanish Theory (extracting more labor) loses wars.** Factor turnover into any productivity equation.
8. **Quality is the builder's self-esteem.** Lowering quality destroys motivation. High quality drives high productivity.
9. **Parkinson's Law doesn't apply to knowledge workers.** The UNSW data: projects with no estimates had the highest productivity.
10. **There are no silver bullets.** The seven sirens of productivity are false hopes. Real improvement comes from culture, environment, and how you treat people.
11. **Management = making it possible for people to work.** Not making them work.
