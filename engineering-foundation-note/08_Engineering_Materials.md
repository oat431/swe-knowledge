# Engineering Materials

Material selection is a critical design decision. Engineers consider properties such as density, strength, flexibility, machinability, durability, thermal expansion, conductivity, corrosion resistance, cost, and repairability. The goal is to find materials that are lighter, stronger, and suited to the application.

---

## Material Selection

When selecting materials for a specific application, engineers ask questions like:

- How strong is the material under expected loads?
- How does it behave at elevated temperatures?
- How much does it expand with temperature change?
- How heavy and flexible is it?
- What are its energy-absorbing properties?
- Does it corrode? How does it react to chemicals?
- How expensive is it? Can it be repaired easily?
- Does it conduct or insulate electricity and heat?

### Bioengineering Considerations

For implants and medical devices, additional factors apply:

- Is the material toxic to the body?
- Can it be sterilized?
- Does it corrode or deteriorate in body fluid?
- How does it respond to mechanical shock and fatigue?
- Are its mechanical properties compatible with bone?

---

## Phases of Matter

Matter exists in four states: **solid**, **liquid**, **gas**, and **plasma**. Phase affects material properties — for example, ice, liquid water, and steam all have different densities.

> Properties depend not only on phase but also on **temperature** and **pressure**. Always account for operating conditions when selecting materials.

## Electrical, Mechanical, and Thermophysical Properties

### Electrical Properties

| Property | Description |
|---|---|
| **Electrical resistivity** | Resistance to flow of electricity. Plastics/ceramics = high; metals = low. Silver and copper are best conductors. |

### Mechanical Properties

| Property | Description |
|---|---|
| **Density** | Mass per unit volume (kg/m³). Aluminum ≈ 2700, Steel ≈ 7850. |
| **Modulus of elasticity** (Young's modulus) | Resistance to stretching/compression. Higher = stiffer. Steel ≈ 190–210 GPa, Aluminum ≈ 70–79 GPa. |
| **Modulus of rigidity** (Shear modulus) | Resistance to twisting/shearing. Steel ≈ 75–80 GPa, Aluminum ≈ 26–36 GPa. |
| **Tensile strength** | Maximum tensile load per unit area before failure. Includes yield strength (elastic limit) and ultimate strength. |
| **Compression strength** | Maximum compressive load before failure. Concrete: 10–70 MPa. |
| **Modulus of resilience** | Energy absorbed without permanent damage. |
| **Modulus of toughness** | Ability to handle overloading before fracture. |
| **Strength-to-weight ratio** | Strength ÷ specific weight. Key for aerospace and structural applications. |

### Thermophysical Properties

| Property | Description |
|---|---|
| **Thermal expansion** | Change in length per unit length per degree temperature change. Critical for structures with large temperature swings. |
| **Thermal conductivity** | Ability to transfer heat from hot to cold regions. |
| **Heat capacity** | Energy to raise 1 kg by 1°C. Higher = better thermal storage. |
| **Viscosity** | Resistance to fluid flow. Water < motor oil < glycerin. |
| **Vapor pressure** | Tendency to evaporate. Low vapor pressure = less evaporation. |
| **Bulk modulus of compressibility** | How easily a fluid's volume can be reduced under pressure. |

> Use **manufacturer's property values** in design calculations. Textbook values are typical, not exact. Properties change with temperature, age, processing, and composition.

## Common Solid Engineering Materials

### Lightweight Metals

**Aluminum, titanium, and magnesium** — called lightweight metals for their low density relative to steel and high strength-to-weight ratios. Widely used in structural and aerospace applications.

#### Aluminum

- Density ≈ one-third that of steel
- Pure aluminum is soft; alloyed with Cu, Zn, Mg, Mn, Si, Li for strength
- Resists corrosion, easy to mill/cut, can be brazed/welded/joined with adhesives
- Good conductor of electricity and heat
- Fabricated as sheets, plates, foil, rods, wire; extruded for frames and automotive parts
- Applications: beverage cans, foil, building insulation, window frames, automotive parts

#### Titanium

- Excellent strength-to-weight ratio; usable up to 400–600°C
- Alloyed with other metals to improve properties; excellent corrosion resistance
- Density ≈ one-half that of steel; more expensive than aluminum
- Applications: gas turbine engine blades (fan and compressor), airplane airframes, landing gear, golf clubs, bicycle frames, tennis racquets, spectacle frames, desalination tubing, hip replacements

#### Magnesium

- Density ≈ 1700 kg/m³ (lighter than aluminum)
- Pure magnesium lacks structural strength; alloyed with Al, Mn, Zn
- Applications: nuclear applications, dry-cell batteries, aerospace, automobile parts, sacrificial anodes for corrosion protection

### Copper and Its Alloys

- Excellent electrical conductor → home wiring, electrical applications
- Good heat conductor → heat exchangers in HVAC systems
- Alloyed with zinc → **brass**; alloyed with tin → **bronze**; alloyed with aluminum → **aluminum bronze**
- Applications: tubes, pipes, fittings, plumbing, heating, hydraulic brake lines, pumps, screws

### Iron and Steel

- **Steel** = iron + ~2% or less carbon. Pure iron is too soft for structural use; carbon hardens it.
- Properties modified by adding chromium, nickel, manganese, silicon, tungsten
- Three broad groups:
  - **Carbon steels** (0.015–2% carbon): most common; appliance bodies, cars
  - **Low-alloy steels** (≤8% alloying elements): machine parts, tool parts, structural members
  - **High-alloy steels** (>8% alloying elements): stainless steels (10–30% Cr, up to 35% Ni). 18/8 stainless = tableware, kitchenware
- **Cast iron** = iron + 2–4% carbon → brittle (unlike ductile low-carbon iron alloys)

### Concrete

- Three ingredients: **aggregate** (gravel, sand), **cement** (binder), **water**
- Water-to-cement ratio affects strength
- Cement-to-aggregate ratio affects strength and durability
- Surrounding temperature during curing affects final strength
- Cold-climate pours: calcium chloride added to cement
- Applications: roads, bridges, buildings, tunnels, dams

## Summary

- **Material selection** depends on mechanical, thermal, electrical, and chemical properties plus cost and manufacturability
- Properties depend on **phase, temperature, pressure, processing, and composition**
- **Mechanical properties** include elasticity, rigidity, tensile/compression strength, resilience, and toughness
- **Thermophysical properties** include thermal expansion, conductivity, heat capacity, viscosity, and vapor pressure
- **Lightweight metals** (Al, Ti, Mg) offer high strength-to-weight ratios for aerospace and structural use
- **Copper alloys** excel in electrical and thermal conductivity
- **Steel** is the workhorse structural material — properties tuned by carbon content and alloying elements
- **Concrete** dominates construction — strength depends on mix ratios and curing conditions
